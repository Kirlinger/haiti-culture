👑 Governance

Haiti's Leaders

From the founding fathers of the nation to contemporary presidents, these men and women have led Haiti through its heights and its trials.

Inicio / Historia / Líderes

1791 – 1820

The Founding Fathers of the Nation

These revolutionary figures led Haiti from colonial slavery to independence, forging the first Black Republic in the world and laying the foundations of a sovereign state.

👑

Toussaint Louverture

1743 – 1803 • Revolutionary Leader, Governor

Born into slavery, Toussaint Louverture became the greatest military strategist of the Haitian Revolution. He successively defeated the Spanish, British, and French armies, then proclaimed himself governor for life in the Constitution of 1801. Captured by Napoleon's treachery, he died in the prison of Fort de Joux in France. His vision and courage paved the way for the independence he did not live to see.

⚔️

Jean-Jacques Dessalines

1758 – 1806 • Emperor Jacques I, Father of the Nation

Born into slavery, Dessalines became the general who defeated the Napoleonic forces at Vertières in 1803. On January 1, 1804, in Gonaïves, he proclaimed Haiti's independence. Becoming Emperor Jacques I, he promulgated the Constitution of 1805 declaring all Haitians "Black" regardless of skin color. Assassinated in October 1806, he remains the undisputed founding father of the Haitian nation.

🏰

Henri Christophe

1767 – 1820 • King Henri I of Northern Haiti

A general of the Revolution, Henri Christophe governed northern Haiti after Dessalines' death. He proclaimed himself King Henri I in 1811 and built the majestic Citadelle Laferrière and the Palais Sans-Souci, now listed as UNESCO World Heritage sites. A visionary administrator, he sought to build a prosperous and sovereign state. In 1820, facing a revolt by his generals, he took his own life in his palace.

🤝

Alexandre Pétion

1770 – 1818 • President of the Republic of the South

After Dessalines' death, Pétion governed the southern part of Haiti as a republican president. A statesman with deep democratic convictions, he implemented a generous agrarian reform distributing land to peasants. He supported Simón Bolívar by providing troops and resources to liberate South America. His generosity and humanism earned him the nickname "Pétion the Great."

1818 – 1915

The 19th Century: Building and Crises

After the deaths of the founding fathers, Haiti endures a century marked by often violent political successions, the heavy debt imposed by France, and attempts at modernization.

📜

Jean-Pierre Boyer

1776 – 1850 • President 1818 – 1843

Pétion's successor, Boyer reunited the two parts of Haiti in 1820 after Christophe's death. In 1822, he annexed the eastern part of the island, uniting all of Hispaniola under a single Haitian government for 22 years. In 1825, under pressure from the French war fleet, he agreed to pay 150 million francs to France in exchange for recognition of Haitian independence — a crushing debt that would weigh on the country for more than a century.

👑

Faustin Soulouque

1782 – 1867 • President then Emperor Faustin I (1847 – 1859)

A military officer born into slavery, Faustin Soulouque became president in 1847 then proclaimed himself Emperor Faustin I in 1849, creating a sumptuous imperial court. His reign was marked by political repression and two unsuccessful attempts to retake the eastern part of the island. Overthrown in 1859, he spent the rest of his life in exile in France.

🏛️

Lysius Salomon

1815 – 1888 • President 1879 – 1888

An intellectual and experienced statesman, Lysius Salomon acceded to the presidency at age 64. His term was marked by the founding of the National Bank of Haiti in 1880 and attempts at economic modernization. He faced, however, violent political resistance. Victim of an insurrection in 1888, he died in exile in Paris a few weeks after his removal from office.

⚖️

Florvil Hyppolite

1828 – 1896 • President 1889 – 1896

Coming to power after a civil war, Florvil Hyppolite presided over a period of relative stability and infrastructure development. His government negotiated with the United States over the naval base at Môle-Saint-Nicolas, a request he firmly refused, defending national sovereignty. He died of a heart attack in 1896 while riding toward rebel troops.

🎖️

Nord Alexis

1820 – 1910 • President 1902 – 1908

A general of 81 years when he came to the presidency, Nord Alexis is one of the last Haitian presidents to have personally known revolutionaries from 1804. His term was marked by relative stability and by the recognition of his great age as a guarantee of wisdom and experience. He was overthrown in 1908 and died two years later in Jamaica.

🚨

Jean Vilbrun Guillaume Sam

Born c. 1859 – 1915 • President February – July 1915

The last Haitian president before the American occupation, Guillaume Sam exercised power for only a few months. Facing a political insurrection, he ordered the massacre of more than 160 political prisoners in Port-au-Prince's jails. In retaliation, an angry crowd lynched him in the French legation where he had taken refuge. His dramatic death served as a pretext for the American military intervention a few days later.

1915 – 1957

Under Occupation and After

From 1915 to 1934, the United States militarily occupies Haiti. Presidents of this period exercise limited power under American tutelage. After the troops depart, Haiti seeks to rebuild its autonomy.

🇺🇸

Philippe Sudre Dartiguenave

1863 – 1926 • President 1915 – 1922

A Haitian senator elected president under direct pressure from American Marines in 1915, Dartiguenave signed the treaty placing Haiti under US tutelage for twenty years. His collaborationist government was sharply criticized by Haitian nationalists. He nevertheless presided over the drafting of a new constitution in 1918, written in part by Franklin D. Roosevelt, then US Assistant Secretary of the Navy.

📋

Louis Borno

1865 – 1942 • President 1922 – 1930

Dartiguenave's successor, Louis Borno governed Haiti for eight years within the framework of the American occupation. A lawyer and cultured man, he developed the country's infrastructure through American investments while restricting political freedoms. His regime faced the repression of nationalist movements. He left power in 1930 under pressure from an American commission that recognized the growing unpopularity of the occupation.

🗝️

Sténio Vincent

1874 – 1959 • President 1930 – 1941

Sténio Vincent is the president who negotiated the definitive departure of American troops in 1934, ending 19 years of occupation. An avowed nationalist, he was hailed as a liberator by the Haitian people. His term was, however, overshadowed by the massacre of tens of thousands of Haitians in the Dominican Republic in 1937 on Trujillo's orders, and by his response judged too moderate in the face of this genocide.

🏗️

Paul Eugène Magloire

1907 – 2001 • President 1950 – 1956

A popular military man, Paul Magloire came to the presidency in 1950 during the first universal male suffrage election in Haiti. His term, marked by relative economic prosperity, saw infrastructure construction and the hosting of the Port-au-Prince Bicentennial Games in 1950. The passage of Hurricane Hazel in 1954 caused heavy damage. Forced into exile in 1956 by a general strike, he returned to live in Haiti in 1986 and died at age 93.

1957 – 1986

The Duvalier Dictatorship

Father and son governed Haiti for 29 years. A period of political terror, corruption, and mass exodus that left deep scars in Haitian society.

💀

François Duvalier

1907 – 1971 • President then President for Life (1957 – 1971)

A country doctor nicknamed "Papa Doc," François Duvalier was elected president in 1957. He quickly established a total dictatorship, relying on the Tonton Macoutes, a paramilitary militia that terrorized the population. In 1964, he proclaimed himself President for Life. His regime had thousands of opponents, intellectuals, and professionals assassinated, tortured, or pushed into exile. He died in 1971 and passed power to his 19-year-old son.

💸

Jean-Claude Duvalier

1951 – 2014 • President for Life (1971 – 1986)

Nicknamed "Baby Doc," Jean-Claude Duvalier inherited power at 19 upon his father's death. His regime, less deadly but deeply corrupt, opened up to tourism and foreign investment while maintaining political repression. Growing poverty, glaring inequalities, and popular resistance led to his overthrow on February 7, 1986. He went into exile in France for 25 years before returning to Haiti in 2011, where he died in 2014.

1986 – Today

The Democratic Era and Its Turbulence

Since the fall of Duvalier, Haiti has sought to build a stable democracy. This path is strewn with obstacles: coups, political crises, natural disasters, and chronic instability.

🪖

Henri Namphy

1932 – 2018 • Head of the National Governing Council (1986 – 1988)

A Haitian army general, Henri Namphy took the head of the National Governing Council (CNG) after Duvalier's flight. His transitional regime was marked by serious political violence, notably the massacre of voters during the November 1987 elections. Briefly president in 1988, he was overthrown by another military coup after only a few months in power.

🎓

Ertha Pascal-Trouillot

1943 – 2023 • Provisional President (1990 – 1991)

The first woman to accede to the presidency of Haiti, Ertha Pascal-Trouillot was a respected jurist and judge. Appointed provisional president in March 1990, she organized the first free and transparent elections in the country's history, won by Jean-Bertrand Aristide. She peacefully transferred power and represented a historic moment para Haitían democracy and for women's rights.

✊

Jean-Bertrand Aristide

Born 1953 • President (1991, 1994 – 1995, 2001 – 2004)

A Catholic priest of liberation theology, Aristide was elected in December 1990 with more than 67% of votes in Haiti's first free elections. Overthrown by a military coup in 1991, he was restored to office in 1994 through international intervention. Re-elected in 2001, he left power in 2004 in controversial circumstances during a new political crisis. He lives today in Haiti.

🌱

René Préval

1943 – 2017 • President (1996 – 2001, 2006 – 2011)

René Préval is the only Haitian president to have completed two full terms and to have peacefully transferred power on two occasions. Discreet and pragmatic, he was first Aristide's ally then his adversary. His second term was dominated by the management of the devastating 2010 earthquake that killed more than 200,000 people. He died in 2017 and remains one of the most respected political figures of the post-Duvalier era.

🎵

Michel Martelly

Born 1961 • President (2011 – 2016)

A popular kompas singer known as "Sweet Micky," Michel Martelly won the presidency in 2011 in the difficult context of post-earthquake reconstruction. His term was marked by the "Ede Pèp" initiative to revive the economy, housing and schooling programs, but also by corruption allegations, political tensions, and a constitutional crisis at the end of his term.

🔴

Jovenel Moïse

1968 – 2021 • President (2017 – 2021)

An agricultural entrepreneur nicknamed "Nèg Bannann" (the banana man), Jovenel Moïse was elected in 2016. His term was marked by massive protests, corruption allegations, a severe economic crisis, and growing insecurity. In the night of July 6–7, 2021, he was assassinated at his home in Port-au-Prince by an armed commando. This crime plunged Haiti into a new spiral of political and security chaos.

🏥

Ariel Henry

Born 1959 • Prime Minister and Transitional Leader (2021 – 2024)

A neurosurgeon and politician, Ariel Henry was appointed Prime Minister by Jovenel Moïse shortly before the latter's assassination. He assumed de facto leadership of the country without being elected, in a context of complete constitutional void. Under his leadership, insecurity linked to armed gangs reached unprecedented levels, notably in 2024. Forced to resign in March 2024 under international pressure and from gangs blocking his return to the country, he gave way to a transitional council.

🌅

Presidential Transitional Council

2024 – Present • Collective Governance

Established in 2024 with the support of the international community, the Presidential Transitional Council brings together representatives of the major Haitian political parties. Its mission is to stabilize the country, support the Multinational Security Support Mission (MSSM) deployed by Kenya and other nations, and prepare conditions for free elections. Haiti has not held an election since 2016.

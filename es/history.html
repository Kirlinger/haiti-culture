The History of Haiti

From the Taino paradise to the first Black Republic in the world, from revolutions to contemporary crises, a history of courage, resistance and resilience spanning thousands of years, right up to today.

Inicio / Historia

A History Like No Other

Haiti's Historical Journey

Haiti's history spans thousands of years, from the Taino people who named the island "Ayiti" (Land of High Mountains) to the revolutionary heroes who defeated Napoleon's army and declared independence on January 1, 1804, to the challenges and extraordinary resilience of the Haitian people today.

Cronología

Key Moments in History

From the Taino civilization to the modern Republic, all the events that shaped Haiti and the world, from its origins to today.

~5000 BC – 1492

The Taino Civilization

The island of Hispaniola had been inhabited for millennia by the Taino people, an Arawakan group from South America. They called the island "Ayiti" (Land of High Mountains) or "Kiskeya" (Mother of All Lands). Organized into five great chiefdoms led by caciques, the Tainos practiced agriculture (cassava, corn, sweet potato), fishing, hunting, and refined craftsmanship. Their population is estimated at between 100,000 and over a million individuals before the arrival of Europeans. They had a rich spirituality based on the worship of zemis (spirits) and a complex social organization.

1492

Arrival of Christopher Columbus

On December 5, 1492, Christopher Columbus landed on the northern coast of Hispaniola and called it "La Española." He founded the first European settlement in the Americas, La Navidad, using the wreckage of the Santa María. This first contact marked the beginning of the end for the Taino civilization. Spanish colonization had begun, heralding an era of brutal destruction for the indigenous populations.

1493 – 1550

Taino Genocide and the Beginning of Slavery

The Spanish enslaved the Tainos for mining and agricultural exploitation. Forced labor, European diseases (smallpox, typhus), and massacres decimated the indigenous population at a catastrophic rate: from over 100,000 inhabitants, only a few thousand Tainos remained by 1514. Queen Anacaona, a courageous chief who sought peace, was captured and hanged by Governor Ovando in 1503. From 1501 onward, the first enslaved Africans were brought to the island to replace the dying Taino workforce. This was the beginning of the transatlantic slave trade in the Americas.

1550 – 1625

Spanish Decline and Buccaneers

With gold mines exhausted, Spain's attention turned to the riches of the American continent. The western part of Hispaniola was gradually abandoned and became a refuge for French, English, and Dutch pirates and buccaneers. The island of Tortuga (Tortugas), off the northwest coast, became a haven for buccaneers — Franco-British hunters who smoked beef and wild pork in the Native American style ("boucan"). These buccaneers would become the founding settlers of the future French colony of Saint-Domingue.

1625 – 1664

French Settlement

French settlers gradually established themselves on the western coast of Hispaniola, particularly on the island of Tortuga (1625), which became the first official base of the French presence. The West India Company took charge of colonization in 1664. Cacao, indigo, and tobacco plantations developed rapidly, driven by an ever-growing enslaved African workforce imported from West Africa.

1697

Treaty of Ryswick, Birth of Saint-Domingue

By the Treaty of Ryswick, Spain officially ceded the western third of Hispaniola to France. The French colony took the name Saint-Domingue. It would become, in the following century, the most productive and most profitable colony in the entire world, nicknamed the "Pearl of the Antilles," through sugar, coffee, indigo, and cotton — all produced by the forced and brutal labor of hundreds of thousands of enslaved Africans.

18th century

Saint-Domingue at Its Peak: The Hell of Sugar

By the late 18th century, Saint-Domingue produced 40% of the sugar and 60% of the coffee consumed in Europe. The colony had approximately 500,000 enslaved people (Africans and Afro-descendants) against 32,000 white colonists and 25,000 free "people of color" (often mixed race). Colonial society was built on a violent racial hierarchy codified by the Code Noir (1685). Living conditions for enslaved people were of unimaginable cruelty: average life expectancy of seven years after arrival, systematic torture, families torn apart. Several slave revolts took place before the great insurrection of 1791, including the rebellion of François Mackandal (1751–1758), a legendary poisoner who terrorized planters before being captured and burned alive.

August 22, 1791

Bwa Kayiman Ceremony: The Fire of Freedom

In the night of August 14–15, 1791, a secret Vodou ceremony took place in the woods of Bwa Kayiman (Bois Caïman). Presided over by the houngan Dutty Boukman and the priestess Cécile Fatiman, it gathered the leaders of the rebel enslaved people. A week later, on August 22, 1791, the revolt erupted simultaneously on dozens of plantations in the north. Within days, more than 1,000 plantations were set ablaze, and the largest armed resistance in the history of slavery had begun. Boukman was captured and beheaded in November 1791, but the flame of revolution never went out again.

1791 – 1793

The Revolution Ignites

The revolution quickly spread throughout the colony. The colonial assembly, torn between large white planters, small white colonists, and free people of color, was unable to cope with the insurrection. France, in the midst of its own Revolution, sent civil commissioners, including Léger-Félicité Sonthonax, to restore order. In August 1793, facing chaos and British and Spanish invasion, Sonthonax proclaimed the abolition of slavery in the colony. This radical decision was confirmed by the French National Convention in February 1794. It was in this context that Toussaint Louverture, previously allied with the Spanish, joined the French side.

1793 – 1801

Toussaint Louverture: The Architect of Freedom

Toussaint Louverture, a former slave turned brilliant military strategist, emerged as the undisputed leader of the revolution. He drove out the British forces (1798) and the Spanish, progressively unified Saint-Domingue under his authority, and governed the colony de facto. In 1801, he had a constitution adopted that abolished slavery in perpetuity, named him governor for life, and established great autonomy from France. Napoleon Bonaparte, then First Consul, saw this as a threat and decided to send a military expedition to restore colonial order and, secretly, the slave regime.

1802 – 1803

Leclerc's Expedition and the Betrayal

In January 1802, General Charles Leclerc, Napoleon's brother-in-law, landed with an army of 20,000 men. After fierce fighting, Toussaint Louverture accepted a truce, but was betrayed, arrested at a meeting under false pretenses in June 1802, and deported to France. He died in the freezing dungeons of Fort de Joux in the Jura mountains in April 1803. Upon learning that Napoleon had restored slavery in Guadeloupe, the Haitian generals — Jean-Jacques Dessalines, Henri Christophe, Alexandre Pétion — took up arms again. Yellow fever decimated the French army (Leclerc himself died from it), making the Haitian victory possible.

November 18, 1803

Battle of Vertières: The Final Victory

The Battle of Vertières, near Cap-Haïtien, was the final great battle of the Haitian Revolution. General Jean-Jacques Dessalines and his troops crushed the French forces of General Rochambeau. It was the first and only time in world history that an army of formerly enslaved people defeated a Napoleonic European army. France capitulated. Haiti's fate was sealed: independence was imminent and inevitable.

January 1, 1804

Independence Declared! 🇭🇹

In Gonaïves, Jean-Jacques Dessalines solemnly proclaimed the independence of Haiti, restoring the ancient Taino name of the island. Haiti thus became the first free Black Republic in the world and the second country in the Americas to gain independence, after the United States. Dessalines tore the white section from the French tricolor flag, keeping only the blue and red, symbolizing the union between Black and mixed-race Haitians, and the definitive break with white colonial domination. This date is one of the most important in the history of humanity: it demonstrated that slavery could be broken through armed resistance and collective organization.

1804 – 1806

The Empire of Dessalines

Dessalines proclaimed himself Emperor of Haiti under the name Jacques I in October 1804. His reign was marked by agrarian reforms, land redistribution, and a radical nationalist policy. The massacre of January–April 1804, during which the great majority of the remaining white population was killed on Dessalines' orders, was motivated by fear of colonial reconquest. Dessalines was assassinated on October 17, 1806 in an ambush at Pont-Rouge, in Port-au-Prince, likely orchestrated by mixed-race officers discontented with his authoritarianism. His death plunged Haiti into a long period of division.

1806 – 1820

A Divided Nation: Two States in One

After Dessalines' assassination, Haiti split into two entities: the north, governed by Henri Christophe (first as president, then as King Henri I from 1811), and the south, governed as a Republic by Alexandre Pétion, then by Jean-Pierre Boyer from 1818. Christophe built grand achievements: the Citadelle Laferrière (1805–1820), an impregnable fortress atop a mountain, and the Palais Sans-Souci (1813), a royal residence of great beauty. Pétion, meanwhile, distributed land to former soldiers and supported Simón Bolívar in his struggle for South American independence. Christophe, ill and abandoned, took his own life in 1820.

1820 – 1843

Boyer: Reunification and Control of the Island

Jean-Pierre Boyer reunified Haiti after Christophe's death in 1820, then, in 1822, invaded and annexed the eastern part of Hispaniola (Santo Domingo), uniting the entire island under Haitian authority for 22 years. In 1825, under the threat of a French war fleet of 14 ships of the line, Boyer signed the ordinance of Charles X recognizing Haiti's independence in exchange for a colossal indemnity of 150 million gold francs (reduced to 90 million in 1838) — a sum representing the equivalent value of property and enslaved people lost by the colonists. Haiti had to borrow from French banks to pay this "independence debt," which would drain its finances for over a century. Boyer governed with increasing autocracy and was overthrown by a revolution in 1843.

1844

Independence of the Dominican Republic

On February 27, 1844, the eastern part of the island revolted against Haitian domination and proclaimed its independence as the Dominican Republic. Haiti attempted to reconquer this territory in several successive wars, but without lasting success. This definitive break divided the island of Hispaniola between two nations with distinct destinies — a situation that persists to this day. Haitian-Dominican relations remained tense throughout the 19th and 20th centuries, marked by discrimination and conflicts.

1844 – 1915

Chronic Instability in the 19th Century

The rest of the 19th century was marked by a rapid succession of governments, coups d'état, and civil wars. Between 1843 and 1915, Haiti had more than 20 presidents, most overthrown or forced into exile. The economy, crushed by the French debt, stagnated. The divisions between the mulatto elites and the Black majority fueled permanent instability. Among the notable figures was Faustin Soulouque (Emperor Faustin I, 1849–1859), who attempted to reconquer Santo Domingo. The independence debt imposed by France was not fully repaid until 1947, 122 years after it was contracted, depriving Haiti of crucial resources for its development throughout that time.

1915 – 1934

American Occupation

On July 28, 1915, American Marines landed in Port-au-Prince, citing political instability and the protection of American economic interests (notably the National Bank of Haiti). The occupation lasted 19 years. The Americans modernized roads, hospitals, and telecommunications, but at the cost of a profound national humiliation: suspension of the constitution, forced labor, racial discrimination, and total subjugation of Haitian sovereignty. The armed resistance of the Cacos, led notably by Charlemagne Péralte (assassinated in 1919), was brutally repressed. The occupation left lasting political and psychological scars, while strengthening a pro-American mulatto elite at the expense of the Black majority.

1934 – 1957

The Post-Occupation Era and the Noiriste Movement

After the Americans departed, Haiti experienced a period of relative stability under several successive presidents. The indigenist and Noiriste movement, championed by intellectuals like Jean Price-Mars, reclaimed the dignity of Black Haitian culture, the Vodou religion, and the African origins of the Haitian people. This cultural and political movement prepared the ground for the rise of François Duvalier. World War II, in which Haiti symbolically participated alongside the Allies, created a period of modest economic growth. But political instability returned after 1945 with a series of military coups.

1957 – 1971

François "Papa Doc" Duvalier: The Dictatorship

François Duvalier, a country doctor nicknamed "Papa Doc," was elected president in 1957. He quickly established a ruthless dictatorship founded on terror and a personality cult. His paramilitary militia, the Tonton Macoutes (National Security Volunteers), spread fear throughout the population, committing extortion, torture, and assassination with total impunity. Duvalier manipulated the Vodou religion to legitimize his power, presenting himself as an incarnation of Baron Samedi, the lwa of death. In 1964, he had himself proclaimed President for Life. Under his rule, tens of thousands of Haitians were killed or forced into exile, draining the country of its intelligentsia. He died in 1971, leaving power to his son Jean-Claude.

1971 – 1986

Jean-Claude "Baby Doc" Duvalier

Jean-Claude Duvalier, nicknamed "Baby Doc," succeeded his father at just 19 years old. Less cruel but equally authoritarian, his reign was marked by massive corruption, superficial economic liberalization, and growing international aid, largely diverted to benefit the elite. The economic and social situation of the Haitian masses continued to deteriorate. In January 1986, a massive popular uprising, supported by community radio stations and the Catholic Church, and international pressure (notably American) forced "Baby Doc" to flee to France on February 7, 1986 aboard an American military aircraft. The end of 29 years of Duvalierist rule.

1986 – 1990

Difficult Transition to Democracy

After the fall of Duvalier, the National Governing Council (CNG), composed of military officials, seized power. The period was marked by violence, aborted elections (notably those of November 29, 1987, interrupted by a massacre of civilians perpetrated by Macoutes), and a succession of provisional governments. The army, reconverted Macoutes, and oligarchs repeatedly attempted to prevent any genuine democratization. It was in this difficult context that the priest Jean-Bertrand Aristide, a liberation theology theologian and defender of the poor ("Titid"), emerged as the voice of the people.

1990

First Democratic Election

On December 16, 1990, Jean-Bertrand Aristide won Haiti's first truly free and democratic presidential elections with 67% of the vote. His election represented an immense hope for the country's poor majority, under the banner of "Lavalas" (the flood that cleanses everything). Aristide took office on February 7, 1991, the anniversary of Duvalier's fall. His government promised deep social reforms and an end to impunity for the crimes of the dictatorship. But the economic elites and part of the army saw him as an existential threat.

1991 – 1994

Military Coup and International Embargo

On September 30, 1991, a military coup led by General Raoul Cédras overthrew Aristide, who was forced into exile. The military regime that followed committed grave human rights violations. The international community responded with a severe economic embargo, which worsened the poverty of the Haitian population far more than it harmed the coup plotters. Under American pressure, and after Clinton threatened a military intervention (Operation "Restore Democracy"), the generals agreed to leave. Aristide returned to the country on October 15, 1994, escorted by American troops, and completed his term until February 1996.

1995 – 2000

René Préval and Democratization

René Préval, Aristide's ally, was elected president in December 1995 and took office in February 1996, marking the first peaceful transition between elected presidents in Haitian history. His term (1996–2001) was marked by the dissolution of the Haitian army (1995), the creation of the Haitian National Police (PNH), and efforts at economic stabilization. Aristide was re-elected in 2000 in elections contested by the opposition. Political tensions between the Lavalas government and the opposition progressively paralyzed the country.

2004

Second Overthrow of Aristide

On February 29, 2004, in the context of Haiti's bicentennial of independence, Aristide was forced out of power a second time — according to him, kidnapped by American soldiers and forcibly transported to Central Africa. Armed groups (ex-military and paramilitaries) had taken control of several cities. A transitional government was put in place, and the UN deployed the United Nations Stabilization Mission in Haiti (MINUSTAH), led by Brazil, which remained until 2017. This period inaugurated a prolonged international presence in Haiti, with mixed results, including a cholera epidemic introduced by Nepalese UN peacekeepers in 2010 that killed nearly 10,000 people.

January 12, 2010

The Devastating Earthquake

A magnitude 7.0 earthquake struck the metropolitan area of Port-au-Prince on January 12, 2010 at 4:53 PM local time. It was one of the most deadly natural disasters in recent history: between 100,000 and 300,000 dead (depending on the source), more than 300,000 injured, 1.5 million homeless. The entire government, parliament, national palace, and critical infrastructure were destroyed. International aid poured in massively (more than $13 billion promised), but reconstruction was slow, chaotic, and tainted by corruption. Displacement camps persisted for years. Despite this unprecedented tragedy, the Haitian people showed remarkable solidarity and resilience in surviving and rebuilding.

2011 – 2015

Michel Martelly and Reconstruction

Michel Martelly, a popular musician nicknamed "Sweet Micky," was elected president in March 2011 in a controversial context, with the support of the United States and the international community. His government (2011–2016) launched reconstruction programs, including the "Ede Pèp" (Help the People) program and free schooling ("Ti Manman Chéri"). However, his term was marked by corruption, the absence of legislative elections for several years, and a progressive deterioration of governance. In 2015–2016, Haiti found itself without a parliament and without valid elections, concentrating all powers in the hands of the executive.

October 2016

Hurricane Matthew: A New Catastrophe

On October 4, 2016, Category 4 Hurricane Matthew struck the southern peninsula of Haiti with winds reaching 230 km/h. The Grand'Anse, Nippes, and Sud departments were devastated: more than 546 dead, 175,000 people displaced, 80% of crops destroyed in some regions. Towns like Jérémie and Les Cayes were almost entirely razed. This catastrophe, occurring six years after the 2010 earthquake, demonstrated Haiti's extreme vulnerability to natural disasters and the inadequacy of reconstruction efforts. The already exhausted country had to face a new humanitarian crisis.

2016 – 2021

Jovenel Moïse: A Deep Political Crisis

Jovenel Moïse, a businessman in the banana industry, was elected president in November 2016 after elections that were annulled and re-held. His term was contested from the start. In 2018, the "PetroCaribe" scandal — the alleged misappropriation of billions of dollars from the Venezuelan oil program — triggered massive demonstrations demanding his resignation. In 2019–2020, popular movements paralyzed the country ("Peyi Lòk"). Moïse governed by presidential decrees after parliament was dissolved, amid growing insecurity fueled by armed gangs that progressively seized entire neighborhoods of Port-au-Prince. The humanitarian situation was deteriorating rapidly.

August 14, 2021

Earthquake of August 14 and Assassination of Moïse

The year 2021 was doubly tragic. On July 7, 2021, President Jovenel Moïse was assassinated at his private home in Pèlerin, Port-au-Prince, overnight by a commando of about twenty armed men, including several Colombian mercenaries. This assassination plunged Haiti into an unprecedented deep political crisis, leaving the country without a legitimate government or elected parliament. A few weeks later, on August 14, 2021, a magnitude 7.2 earthquake struck the southern department, destroying more than 130,000 homes, killing more than 2,200 people and injuring 12,000 others. Tropical Storm Grace struck two days later, further worsening the destruction. A large-scale humanitarian crisis was layered on top of the political crisis.

2021 – 2023

Ariel Henry and the Rise of the Gangs

Ariel Henry, appointed Prime Minister by Moïse just hours before his assassination, took de facto power. Without elections or a parliament, his government lacked legitimacy. In this institutional void, armed gangs, grouped into coalitions such as G9 (led by Jimmy Chérizier, known as "Barbecue") and later "Viv Ansanm," extended their control over Port-au-Prince, seizing the main roads, markets, and residential neighborhoods. Kidnapping became endemic. The economy collapsed. Millions of Haitians were forced to flee to other regions or abroad. Humanitarian organizations faced growing difficulty in reaching populations in need.

February – March 2024

Gang Offensive: State Paralysis

In February 2024, a gang coalition ("Viv Ansanm") launched an unprecedented coordinated offensive against state institutions. Prisons were attacked, freeing more than 4,000 detainees. The international Toussaint Louverture airport was besieged and closed. The port of Port-au-Prince was blocked. Police stations and courthouses were set on fire. The gangs controlled up to 80–95% of the Port-au-Prince metropolitan area. Ariel Henry, on a trip to Kenya to sign a security force deployment agreement, was prevented from returning to Haiti. On March 12, 2024, he announced his resignation, following negotiations with CARICOM and international pressure. It was the de facto capitulation of the Haitian state before armed groups.

2024 – 2025

Presidential Transitional Council and Hope for Stability

After Ariel Henry's resignation, a Presidential Transitional Council (CPT) composed of representatives of the main political sectors and civil society was installed in April 2024. Garry Conille was appointed Prime Minister in June 2024, before being replaced by Alix Didier Fils-Aimé in November 2024. Kenya, leading a UN-backed Multinational Security Support (MSS) mission, deployed hundreds of police officers to Haiti. The Mission worked to retake key areas in Port-au-Prince. On the humanitarian front, 44% of the Haitian population faced acute food insecurity, and more than 700,000 people were internally displaced. Despite an extremely difficult situation, Haitian voices — young people, artists, civil society actors — continued to carry the hope of a sovereign, just, and free Haiti, faithful to the revolutionary heritage of 1804.

Revolutionary Heroes

Founding Fathers & Heroes

The men and women who fought para Haití's freedom and shaped a nation that has inspired freedom movements around the world.

🦁

Toussaint Louverture

Revolutionary General

Born into slavery, Toussaint became one of the greatest military commanders of his era. He defeated the armies of France, Great Britain, and Spain and drafted the first Haitian constitution in 1801.

🦅

Jean-Jacques Dessalines

Founding Father & First Emperor

Dessalines led the final battle for independence and declared Haiti a free nation on January 1, 1804. He took the Haitian flag and tore out the white section, symbolizing the removal of white colonial domination.

👑

Henri Christophe

King of Northern Haiti

Henri Christophe built the Citadelle Laferrière and the Palais Sans-Souci, monumental achievements that demonstrated Haiti's capacity for greatness and self-determination.

🔥

Boukman Dutty

Revolutionary Leader

A Vodou houngan and enslaved leader who presided over the Bwa Kayiman ceremony in August 1791, calling for resistance and igniting the Haitian Revolution.

⚡

Alexandre Pétion

President of Southern Haiti

Pétion aided Simón Bolívar's liberation of South America, asking only that Bolívar abolish slavery in the liberated territories, spreading Haiti's revolutionary ideals throughout the hemisphere.

🌟

Marie-Claire Heureuse

First Lady & Revolutionary

Wife of Jean-Jacques Dessalines, known for her compassion and humanitarian acts during the revolution. She protected prisoners and cared for the wounded on both sides of the conflict.

🌍

Global Impact

How Haiti Changed the World

The Haitian Revolution of 1804 was not just a victory for one nation — it was a turning point in human history. It proved that enslaved people could defeat a European military power and establish their own free nation.

  • Inspired freedom movements throughout Latin America and the Caribbean
  • Helped fund Simón Bolívar's South American independence campaigns
  • Challenged the institution of slavery throughout the world
  • Created the only nation founded by a successful slave revolution
  • Influenced the abolitionist movement in Europe and North America

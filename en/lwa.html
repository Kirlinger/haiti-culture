✨ Sacred Spirits

The Lwa of Vodou

The Lwa are the intermediary spirits of Haitian Vodou, personified divine forces that govern the domains of nature, human life and the cosmos. Each Lwa has its own personality, colors, songs, vèvè and spheres of power.

The Guardians of the Cosmos

Who Are the Lwa?

The Lwa (also written Loa or Loas) are divine spirits who serve as intermediaries between Bondye (the supreme God) and humans. Each Lwa governs a particular domain of life and nature, and can be contacted during ceremonies to offer protection, guidance, healing and blessing.

Papa Legba

Guardian of Crossroads, Opener of Gates

ColorsBlack & Red
Sacred dayMonday
Associated saintSaint Peter
OfferingsRoasted corn, candy, tobacco, rum

Description & Personality

Papa Legba is the first and most important of all the Lwa; he is always invoked first in any Vodou ceremony. Without his permission, no communication with the other Lwa is possible. He is the guardian of crossroads, gates, passages and the boundaries between the world of the living and that of the spirits.

Legba is depicted as a frail old man leaning on a staff (crutch), wearing a straw hat, a cloth bag and smoking a pipe. Despite his fragile appearance, he is of immense wisdom and power. God of language, communication and understanding, he is also the master of tongues — it is said he speaks all human and spiritual languages.

His equivalent in the Fon religion of Dahomey is Legba, guardian of the entrances to houses and villages. In Brazilian Candomblé he corresponds to Exu. In American popular culture, the legend of the "devil at the crossroads" (popularized by blues music) finds its roots in the figure of Legba.

Role in the Revolution

During the Bwa Kayiman ceremony in 1791, which triggered the Haitian Revolution, Papa Legba was one of the first Lwa invoked, to "open the gates" of freedom. Haitian revolutionaries invoked Legba before each battle, asking him to open the path to victory.

His Vèvè

Legba's vèvè represents a simple cross with staffs and radiating forms at its extremities, symbolizing the four directions and all possible passages. Legba's cross is the first one drawn in any ceremony.

🚪

Sacred Song

« Atibòn Legba, louvri baryè a pou mwen,
Papa Legba, louvri baryè a pou mwen,
Agò ! »
, Hymn to Papa Legba

(Atibòn Legba, open the gate for me,
Papa Legba, open the gate for me,
Make way! I am passing!)

Damballah Wedo

The Great Celestial Serpent, Father of All the Lwa

ColorsWhite & Silver
Sacred dayThursday
Associated saintSaint Patrick
OfferingsWhite eggs, milk, white flour, silver

Description & Personality

Damballah Wedo is one of the oldest and most venerated of all the Lwa. Represented in the form of a great white serpent (or a rainbow), he is considered the primordial father of all the Lwa and the ultimate symbol of purity, wisdom and cosmic unity.

When Damballah possesses a devotee during a ceremony, the person climbs trees, crawls on the ground and hisses, imitating the behavior of a serpent. Damballah does not speak and does not communicate in human words, for he is too primordial, too ancient for language. His presence is felt as a deep peace and purity.

His origin traces back to the serpent god Da (or Dan) of the Fon tradition of Dahomey, representing the cosmic rainbow that encircles the Earth. In Haitian Vodou, Damballah is inseparable from his wife Ayida Wedo; together they symbolize the union of opposites — the masculine and feminine principle, rain and rainbow.

Symbolism

Damballah's serpent symbolizes the continuity of life, the connection between sky and earth, and ancestral wisdom. The serpent's coils represent the cycles of time and reincarnation. His association with silver and white eggs reflects his pure and creative nature — eggs symbolizing the potential of all life, silver reflecting the light of the moon and stars.

🐍

Sacred Song

« Damballah Wedo, ayibobo !
Ou se papa tout Lwa yo,
Serpent dlo ki nan syèl la,
Beni nou, Damballah ! »
, Hymn to Damballah

(Damballah Wedo, glory to you!
You are the father of all the Lwa,
Water serpent who is in the sky,
Bless us, Damballah!)

Rada Nation

Ayida Wedo

The Celestial Rainbow, Wife of Damballah

ColorsWhite, Rainbow
Sacred dayThursday
Associated saintOur Lady of the Immaculate Conception
OfferingsWhite milk, white syrup, eggs, white cotton

Description & Personality

Ayida Wedo is the rainbow Lwa, the divine wife and inseparable companion of Damballah Wedo. Represented as a rainbow or a white female serpent that encircles the sky, she is the embodiment of grace, purity and celestial fertility. If Damballah is the serpent of the earth and rivers, Ayida Wedo is the serpent of the sky and clouds.

Together, Damballah and Ayida Wedo form a complementary pair representing cosmic harmony: water and rainbow, rain and light, masculine and feminine. Their union symbolizes the perfect balance of the universe.

Like Damballah, Ayida Wedo is pure and silent during possession. She is regarded as a force of protection, particularly for pregnant women and newborns. Her cult is intimately linked to water, rain and the fertility of the earth.

Symbolism

The rainbow that appears after rain is considered the visible manifestation of Ayida Wedo traversing the sky. Seeing a rainbow is a sign of her presence and blessing. In Haiti, rainbows are welcomed with respect and gratitude, as a visit from this great Lwa.

Sacred Song

« Ayida Wedo, koulev ak plim blan,
Ou vole nan syèl la tankou yon zanj,
Ayida ! Ayida ! Vini beni pitit ou yo ! »
, Hymn to Ayida Wedo

(Ayida Wedo, serpent with white feathers,
You fly in the sky like an angel,
Ayida! Ayida! Come bless your children!)

Nago / Petro Nation

Ogou Feray

God of War and Iron, Protector of Soldiers

ColorsRed & Black
Sacred dayTuesday
Associated saintSaint James the Great
OfferingsRum, cigars, bananas, red candle

Description & Personality

Ogou (or Ogu, Ogun) is the Lwa of war, iron, blacksmiths, soldiers and politics. He is one of the most powerful and most popular figures of Haitian Vodou, deeply linked to the history of the Haitian Revolution. Revolutionary heroes such as Toussaint Louverture and Jean-Jacques Dessalines were considered servants of Ogou.

Depicted as a proud soldier brandishing a sword or saber, wearing a red military uniform, Ogou is ardent, courageous, sometimes hot-tempered but always just. His possession is spectacular: the mounted person walks like a proud military officer, waves a sword, shouts warrior imprecations and demands rum and cigars.

Ogou is also the patron of blacksmiths and all those who work with metal and fire. He represents the transformative force of fire, capable of both destroying and creating, like iron that must be melted to become a weapon or a tool.

Aspects of Ogou

Ogou has many aspects: Ogou Feray (the ardent warrior), Ogou Badagris (the diplomat and negotiator), Ogou Saint-Jacques (associated with the Catholic saint), Ogou Balindjo (the wise healer), and others. Each aspect represents a different facet of war, power and justice.

Sacred Song

« Ogou, Ogou Feray !
Ou se yon grenn fèy pou n travay !
Fè solda nou yo vin fò,
Ogou, pwoteje pitit ou yo ! »
, Hymn to Ogou

(Ogou, Ogou Feray!
You are a leaf that makes us work!
Make our soldiers strong,
Ogou, protect your children!)

Erzulie Freda

Goddess of Love, Beauty and Luxury

ColorsPink & Gold
Sacred dayTuesday & Thursday
Associated saintMater Dolorosa / Virgin Mary
OfferingsChampagne, perfume, jewelry, pink flowers, cake

Description & Personality

Erzulie Freda is the Lwa of romantic love, beauty, luxury, desires and dreams. She is often depicted as a beautiful mulatto or Creole woman richly dressed, adorned with jewelry, perfumes and flowers. Her symbol is the heart pierced by a sword, symbol of the Catholic Mater Dolorosa she embodies, representing beauty inseparable from pain and unsatisfied desire.

When Erzulie Freda mounts someone during a ceremony, the person begins to weep profusely toward the end of the possession, for Erzulie always weeps — a symbol of her eternal sadness that the world can never be beautiful enough, pure enough, perfect enough to satisfy her infinite desires.

She is capricious and demanding: she requires the finest offerings (champagne, jewelry, Chanel perfumes), spends a long time perfuming herself during possession, arranges her hair coquettishly and dresses in her favorite gowns (kept in the Hounfor). She is the patroness of artists, lovers, women and all those who seek beauty in life.

Erzulie and Haiti

Erzulie Freda is considered the spiritual patroness of Haiti itself. The Haitian nation is sometimes described as her "husband" — she loves Haiti with a passionate love, and her eternal grief reflects the suffering of the country she adores but can never protect enough. This metaphor is deeply embedded in the Haitian consciousness.

💗

Sacred Song

« Erzulie, oh ! Maîtresse !
Ou bèl tankou solèy la,
Kè ou koupe ak doulè,
Men ou renmen nou tout pou toujou ! »
, Hymn to Erzulie Freda

(Erzulie, oh! Mistress!
You are beautiful as the sun,
Your heart is cut by pain,
But you love us all forever!)

Petro Nation

Erzulie Dantor

The Warrior Mother, Protector of Children and Women

ColorsBlue & Gold
Sacred dayTuesday
Associated saintOur Lady of Częstochowa (Black Madonna)
OfferingsClairin (cane spirit), grilled pork, cigarettes, liquid bluing

Description & Personality

Erzulie Dantor is the dark and warrior aspect of the goddess Erzulie. Unlike Freda, who is gentle and romantic, Dantor is fierce, protective, passionate and sometimes dangerous. She is depicted as a dark-skinned woman carrying a dagger, with scars on her face — the legacy of wounds received during the Haitian Revolution, according to tradition.

Dantor is intimately tied to the ceremony of Bwa Kayiman and the Haitian Revolution. It is said that she was the first Lwa invoked on that fateful night of 1791, and that she was wounded in the fighting (hence her scars) but gave her strength to Boukman and the revolutionaries. This is why she cannot speak during possession — she emits only guttural sounds, her tongue having been cut out to punish her for betraying secrets (according to legend).

She is the patroness of single women (single mothers), abandoned children, LGBTQ+ people and all those excluded from society. Her devotion is particularly strong in southern Haiti and among the Haitian diaspora.

Dantor and Freda

Erzulie Dantor and Erzulie Freda are described as rivals — they compete for the same men and cannot be invoked on the same evening in a ceremony. Their rivalry symbolizes the duality of femininity: grace and strength, romance and reality, desire and motherhood.

Sacred Song

« Ezili Dantò, ou pa pale,
Men ou goumen pou pitit ou yo,
Grann Ezili, Mawozo !
Pwoteje fanm ak timoun yo ! »
, Hymn to Erzulie Dantor

(Erzulie Dantor, you do not speak,
But you fight for your children,
Great Erzulie, Mawozo!
Protect the women and children!)

Gede Nation

Baron Samedi

Master of Death, Guardian of Cemeteries

ColorsBlack & Purple
Sacred daySaturday & November 2nd
Associated saintSaint Gerard / Saint Expedit
OfferingsRum with 21 peppers, cigars, peanuts, corn

Description & Personality

Baron Samedi is the most famous and most recognizable Lwa of Haitian Vodou in the entire world. Master of death and cemeteries, he is represented as a skeleton or a man wearing a black top hat, sunglasses with one lens often cracked or missing, a black coat and a perpetually lit cigar.

Contrary to the terrifying image his role suggests, Baron Samedi is extremely facetious, vulgar, obscene and hilarious. His possession is one of the most spectacular in Vodou: the mounted person tells bawdy jokes, eats burning hot peppers, drinks vast quantities of pepper rum and dances obscenely, all in a drawling southern Haitian accent.

Baron Samedi has the power to cure any illness and recover the dying, but only if he chooses to. For he alone decides who dies and who lives. If Baron Samedi refuses to dig someone's grave, that person cannot die. He is the ultimate healer as much as the personification of death.

Baron and the Gede Family

Baron Samedi is the head of the vast Gede family. His wife is Gran Brigitte (Maman Brigitte). Together they reign over all the spirits of the dead. The Gede are particularly active during the Feast of the Dead (November 1st and 2nd), when they invade ceremonies, cemeteries and the streets of Haiti.

💀

Sacred Song

« Bawon Samdi, ou mèt simityè a,
Ou konnen tout mò yo pa ou,
Lè ou ri, zo yo fè bri,
Bawon ! Bawon ! Bawon Lacwa ! »
, Hymn to Baron Samedi

(Baron Samedi, you are the master of the cemetery,
You know all the dead who belong to you,
When you laugh, the bones make noise,
Baron! Baron! Baron of the Cross!)

Gran Brigitte

Baron's Wife, Guardian of Bones and the Dead

ColorsBlack & Purple
Sacred daySaturday & November 2nd
Associated saintSaint Brigid of Ireland
OfferingsPepper rum, strong spirits, medicinal herbs

Description & Personality

Gran Brigitte (also called Maman Brigitte) is the wife of Baron Samedi and the queen of the spirits of the dead (Gede). She is one of the rare Lwa of European origin integrated into the Vodou pantheon — some scholars suggest a connection with Saint Brigid of Ireland, adopted by the Irish and Scottish slaves who worked on the plantations of Saint-Domingue.

Gran Brigitte is often represented as a woman with dark or golden skin and green or grey eyes — an unusually mixed-race appearance in the Vodou pantheon. She protects the graves of the dead in cemeteries, especially those marked with a cross or a Gede symbol.

Like her husband Baron, Gran Brigitte is a great healer, specializing in women's ailments and skin conditions. She is also known for her vulgar language and dark humor during her possessions. She is the keeper of the wisdom of the dead — the secrets that only the departed know.

Sacred Song

« Gran Brijit, manman mò yo,
Ou veye zo nou nan tè a,
Ou konnen sekrè lanmò,
Beni nou, Manman Brijit ! »
, Hymn to Gran Brigitte

(Gran Brigitte, mother of the dead,
You watch over our bones in the earth,
You know the secrets of death,
Bless us, Maman Brigitte!)

Azaka Mede

God of Agriculture, Patron of Haitian Peasants

ColorsBlue & White (chambray)
Sacred dayWednesday
Associated saintSaint Isidore the Farmer
OfferingsTafia (cane spirit), corn, beans, mangoes, clay pipe

Description & Personality

Azaka Mede (also known simply as Zaka or Cousin Zaka) is the Lwa of agriculture, the earth, peasants and fieldwork. He embodies the rural Haitian soul — simple, honest, hard-working and deeply connected to the land. He is the patron of farmers, market vendors and the peasants (peyizan) of Haiti.

Azaka is depicted as a typical Haitian peasant dressed in blue chambray (the fabric of peasants), wearing a woven straw hat, a pipe and a sisal bag (makout) — the same bag used by peasants to carry their goods to market. He speaks with the rural accent of the Haitian Central Plateau.

During possession, Azaka behaves like a peasant: he is suspicious of strangers, demands his offerings (particularly the pipe and tafia), haggles fiercely, accepts little for free, and grumbles if not respected. He is also generous with those who treat him well, granting agricultural prosperity and abundance.

Azaka and Work

Azaka represents the dignity of manual labor and the respect owed to the earth. In a Haitian society where peasants constitute the majority of the population, his veneration is an act of cultural dignity — honoring those who feed the nation and whose daily work is often invisible.

Sacred Song

« Kouzin Zaka, kouzin Mede,
Ou travay tè a pou nou,
Rekòt la dous, paske ou la,
Kouzin Zaka, mèsi ! »
, Hymn to Azaka

(Cousin Zaka, cousin Mede,
You work the earth for us,
The harvest is sweet because you are here,
Cousin Zaka, thank you!)

Agwe Tawoyo

King of the Sea, Protector of Sailors and Fishermen

ColorsBlue & Green (marine)
Sacred daySunday
Associated saintSaint Ulrich / Saint Andrew
OfferingsFish, rum, champagne, white sheep, miniature boat

Description & Personality

Agwe Tawoyo (also spelled Agwe or Agwé) is the Lwa of the sea, oceans, fish and boats. He is the undisputed king of all salt waters and the protector of those who live from the sea or cross it. For a Haitian people whose survival has always been tied to the sea — whether through fishing, trade or diaspora — Agwe is of fundamental importance.

Agwe is depicted as an elegant naval officer, light or green-skinned, with eyes the color of the ocean, wearing a captain's uniform with gold braid. He is the husband of Erzulie Freda and La Sirène (the mermaid of the seas); his two divine wives reflect the two aspects of marine femininity: human love and mysterious depths.

The most spectacular ceremony in his honor is the Manger-Yam or Barque d'Agwe: a miniature boat loaded with offerings (food, rum, perfumes, figurines) is taken out to sea and sunk in his honor. Devotees stand on boats, weeping and singing as the sacred vessel sinks beneath the waves toward his kingdom.

Agwe and the Diaspora

For the millions of Haitians who have crossed the sea (to the United States, Canada, France, the Dominican Republic), Agwe is an essential protector. Ceremonies in his honor are performed by the Haitian diaspora worldwide before any major sea voyage.

Sacred Song

« Agwe, mèt dlo salé,
Ou mennen nou sou lanmè a,
Pwoteje bato nou yo,
Agwe Tawoyo, pa kite nou neye ! »
, Hymn to Agwe

(Agwe, master of the salt water,
You guide us on the sea,
Protect our boats,
Agwe Tawoyo, do not let us drown!)

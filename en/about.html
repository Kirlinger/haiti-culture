About Haiti

A crossroads of African, French and indigenous Taino influences, Haiti is a nation of extraordinary cultural richness, remarkable resilience and immense creative talent.

Who We Are

A Nation of Extraordinary People

Haiti, officially the Republic of Haiti (République d'Haïti / Repiblik Ayiti), is a Caribbean country on the western part of the island of Hispaniola. With over 11 million inhabitants, it is the most populous country in the Caribbean.

The Haitian people are descended from enslaved Africans, indigenous Taino peoples and French settlers. This blend created a culture that is uniquely Haitian — expressive, resilient and deeply spiritual.

Port-au-Prince is the capital and largest city. The country is officially bilingual, with Haitian Creole (Kreyòl ayisyen) spoken by the entire population and French used in official and educational contexts.

Language & Expression

Kreyòl, The Language of the Heart

Haitian Creole is far more than a language — it is an identity, a form of resistance, and living proof of Haiti's unique history.

🗣️

Haitian Creole

Spoken by 100% of Haitians, Kreyòl evolved from African languages, French, Spanish and English. It became an official language in 1987.

📚

French Language

French remains an official language used in government, education and business — a reminder of Haiti's colonial history.

✍️

Literature

Haiti has a rich literary tradition. Authors such as Jacques Roumain, René Depestre and Edwidge Danticat have achieved international recognition.

📢

Oral Tradition

Stories, proverbs (pwovèb) and oral tales have preserved Haitian culture across generations. "Krik ? Krak !" opens every story.

🎶

Sound & Rhythm

Music, The Soul of Haiti

Music is inseparable from Haitian life. From sacred Vodou ceremonies to packed dance floors, rhythm runs through everything Haitian.

  • Kompa — Haiti's beloved popular music genre, born in the 1950s
  • Rara — Street festival music tied to Lenten celebrations
  • Rasin (Roots) — Spiritual music blending Vodou and rock
  • Twoubadou — Romantic Haitian folk music with guitar
  • Méringue — The traditional national music genre

Notable artists include Coupé Cloué, Ti Paris, Tabou Combo and Wyclef Jean, who have brought Haitian sounds to a worldwide audience.

🎨

Visual Arts

Haitian Art, A World Treasure

Haiti's visual arts scene is celebrated internationally. Haitian paintings are known for their vivid colors, spiritual themes and striking portrayals of everyday life.

The Centre d'Art, founded in Port-au-Prince in 1944, launched the modern Haitian art movement. Haitian naïve art attracted worldwide attention when artists such as Hector Hyppolite and Philomé Obin were discovered.

  • Naïve / primitivist painting tradition
  • Metal sculpture using recycled oil drums
  • Papier-mâché masks and carnival arts
  • Vèvè — sacred Vodou symbols used in art
  • Contemporary Haitian artists exhibited worldwide

Food & Flavors

Haitian Cuisine

Bold spices, fresh ingredients and African culinary traditions combine to create a cuisine that reflects Haiti's rich heritage.

🍖

National Dish

Griyo (Fried Pork)

Marinated pork shoulder fried crispy and served with pikliz (spiced pickled vegetables) and rice. Haiti's beloved national dish.

🍚

Staple Food

Diri ak Djon Djon

Black mushroom rice, one of Haiti's most prized dishes, made with unique djon djon mushrooms that give the rice its distinctive dark color.

🌶️

Condiment

Pikliz

The essential Haitian condiment — a fiery pickled blend of cabbage, carrots and Scotch bonnet peppers in vinegar. Found on every Haitian table.

Spirituality

Faith & Vodou, The Spiritual Heart

Religion is at the heart of Haitian life. The majority of Haitians are Catholic or Protestant, but many also practice Vodou — a spiritual system brought from West Africa that played a key role in the Revolution of 1791.

Haitian Vodou (not to be confused with Hollywood voodoo) is a rich and complex religion that honors ancestors, spirits (Lwa) and the divine through music, dance, ritual and ceremony.

  • Bossou, Erzulie, Papa Legba — revered Lwa spirits
  • Bwa Kayiman ceremony (1791) — spark of the Revolution
  • Vodou officially recognized as a religion in 2003
  • Deep connection with healing, community and nature

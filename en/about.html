The researcher behind this project

Kirlinger Jeune

K

Independent researcher on Haitian history

This project was born out of frustration. Every time I searched for serious information about Haiti online, I would find either newspaper articles focused on crises, incomplete Wikipedia pages, or academic books in English inaccessible to the general public. There was no place that brought together Haitian sources, in French, with real documentary rigor.

This project started as a simple personal notebook. I began reading Madiou, then Ardouin, then Trouillot. I realized that these authors — though fundamental — were absent from most online debates about Haiti. The site you are reading is my attempt to correct that, modestly.

I am neither a credentialed historian nor an academic. I am someone who has read a great deal, who takes sources seriously, and who believes that Haiti’s history deserves better than what it usually gets. I am sometimes wrong — if you spot an error or a missing source, report it. I correct.

One thing that bothers me in many writings about Haiti: the obsession with “resilience”. As if suffering with dignity were a sufficient quality. Haiti doesn’t need to be admired for its resilience — it deserves to be understood for its history.

Who We Are

A Nation of Extraordinary People

Haiti, officially the Republic of Haiti (République d’Haïti / Repiblik Ayiti), is a Caribbean country on the western part of the island of Hispaniola. With more than 11 million inhabitants, it is the most populous country in the Caribbean.

The Haitian people are descended from enslaved Africans, indigenous Taíno peoples and French colonists. This mixture created a culture that is uniquely Haitian, expressive, resilient and deeply spiritual.

Port-au-Prince is the capital and largest city. The country is officially bilingual, with Haitian Creole (Kreyòl ayisyen) spoken by the entire population and French used in official and educational contexts.

Language & Expression

Kreyòl — a language, a resistance

Haitian Creole is not “simplified French.” It is a fully-fledged language, born of necessity. Enslaved people from dozens of different peoples — Fon, Yoruba, Wolof, Kongo — forged a common language in the plantations of Saint-Domingue. This process of linguistic creation is itself an act of collective resistance.

For a long time, Kreyòl had no official status. French was the language of the elites, the administration, the courts. It was only with the 1987 constitution that Kreyòl was recognized as an official language co-equal with French. The transformation is still incomplete.

It is significant that the work of Price-Mars (who championed Kreyòl from 1928) took decades to assert itself in Haitian institutions themselves. Linguistic decolonization is a long process.

The oral tradition is equally fundamental. “Krik?” says the storyteller. “Krak!” replies the audience. This ritual opening of tales (kont) spans centuries and connects today’s Haitians to African ancestors.

Two official languages

Kreyòl ayisyen — spoken by 100 % of the population. Language of daily life, music and neighborhood living.

French — language of part of the administration and education. Mastered by a minority. Its institutional dominance is a colonial legacy that the 1987 constitution began to challenge.

Sound & Rhythm

Music, The Soul of Haiti

Music is inseparable from Haitian life. From sacred Vodou ceremonies to packed dance floors, rhythm runs through everything Haitian.

  • Kompa, Haiti’s beloved popular music genre, born in the 1950s
  • Rara, Street festival music linked to Lenten celebrations
  • Rasin (Roots), Spiritual music blending Vodou and rock
  • Twoubadou, Romantic Haitian folk music with guitar
  • Méringue, The traditional national music genre

Notable artists include Coupé Cloué, Ti Paris, Tabou Combo and Wyclef Jean, who have brought Haitian sounds to a worldwide audience.

Visual Arts

Haitian Art, A World Treasure

The Haitian visual arts scene is celebrated internationally. Haitian paintings are known for their vivid colors, spiritual themes and striking depictions of daily life.

The Centre d’Art, founded in Port-au-Prince in 1944, launched the modern Haitian art movement. Haitian naïve art attracted worldwide attention when artists such as Hector Hyppolite and Philomé Obin were discovered.

  • Naïve / primitivist painting tradition
  • Metal sculpture using recycled oil drums
  • Papièr-mâché masks and carnival arts
  • Vèvè, Sacred Vodou symbols used in art
  • Contemporary Haitian artists exhibited worldwide

Food & Flavors

Haitian Cuisine

Bold spices, fresh ingredients and African culinary traditions combine to create a cuisine that reflects Haiti’s rich heritage.

National Dish

Griyo (Fried Pork)

Pork shoulder is marinated for hours in a blend of spices, lime juice and chili peppers, then braised before being fried until golden and crispy. Served with rice and beans and pikliz, griyo is the quintessential national dish, present at every Haitian celebration, baptism or family gathering.

Its preparation is a ritual in itself: every family jealously guards its own marinade recipe, passed down from generation to generation.

Other must-tries

  • Diri ak Djon Djon
    Rice made with black mushrooms endemic to northern Haiti, which dye the grain a deep black and give it a unique earthy aroma. One of the country’s most prized dishes, once reserved for special occasions.
  • Pikliz
    The essential condiment: cabbage, carrots and Scotch bonnet peppers macerated in vinegar. No Haitian table is without it — it enhances griyo as much as vegetables or grilled chicken.

Spirituality

Faith & Vodou, The Spiritual Heart

Religion is at the heart of Haitian life. The majority of Haitians are Catholic or Protestant, but many also practice Vodou, a spiritual system brought from West Africa that played a key role in the Revolution of 1791.

Haitian Vodou (not to be confused with Hollywood voodoo) is a rich and complex religion that honors ancestors, spirits (Lwa) and the divine through music, dance, ritual and ceremony.

  • Bossou, Erzulie, Papa Legba, venerated Lwa spirits
  • Bwa Kayíman ceremony (1791), spark of the Revolution
  • Vodou officially recognized as a religion in 2003
  • Deep connection with healing, community and nature

“Nou se tout Ayiti” — We are all Haiti. The Haitian people carry their culture as a banner of pride, resilience and unshakeable dignity.

, Haitian Proverb

Academic Purpose

An Independent Historical Research Project

This site is an independent historical research project dedicated to documenting the political, constitutional, economic and social history of Haiti through Haitian authors, verified historical sources and academic literature.

Priority sources are Haitian historians and intellectuals: Thomas Madiou, Beaubrun Ardouin, Jean Price-Mars, Michel-Rolph Trouillot, Hénock Trouillot, Jean Fouchard, Jacques Nicolas Léger, Jean Casimir, Suzy Castor and Laënnec Hurbon. This approach aims to restore Haiti’s history as it was lived, analyzed and transmitted by the Haitian people themselves.

Historical events are documented with precise dates, bibliographic references and, where possible, references to official archives (National Archives of Haiti, Library of Congress, constitutions, treaties).

Methodology

Approach and Documentary Rigor

Each section of this site strives to respect the following principles:

  • Priority to Haitian sources — Haitian authors are cited first.
  • Factual accuracy — Dates, names and events are verified by cross-referencing.
  • Historical contextualization — Every fact is placed in its political, economic and social context.
  • Explicit references — Important claims refer back to identified works or archives.
  • Avoiding anachronism — History is presented according to academic standards, without retrospective judgments.

This project is part of the tradition of Haitian intellectual indigenism, inaugurated notably by Jean Price-Mars with Thus Spoke the Uncle (1928), which affirms that knowledge of Haiti must be produced and carried by Haitian voices.

Memory and Identity

Introduction to the Historical Archives

These digital archives bring together founding documents, historical texts and essential speeches related to Haiti. These texts form the foundation of Haitian identity and memory. They bear witness to the struggle for freedom, the building of the state and the voice of those who shaped the nation.

Founding Texts

Historical Documents

These digital archives gather the texts that founded the Haitian nation. The Act of Independence of January 1, 1804, signed by Dessalines and the revolutionary leaders, remains the most emblematic document — its original was presumed lost for two centuries before a copy was rediscovered in 2010 at the British National Archives. The corpus extends from the Constitution of 1801 of Toussaint Louverture, the first fundamental law to abolish slavery throughout the territory, to the Constitution of 1805 of Dessalines, which declared all Haitians “Black” regardless of their skin color — a deliberate and radical break with colonial racist ideology.

Also included are the Act of Gonaïves (November 29, 1803), which foreshadowed independence one month before its official proclamation, the Proclamation of Boisrond-Tonnerre written with acknowledged revolutionary virulence, and the major speeches of Jean-Jacques Dessalines calling for national unity and Haitian pride. Together, these six texts form the irreplaceable foundation of Haitian memory and identity.

Voice of the Nation

Speeches and Important Texts

Henri Christophe — Speech on Nation Building (1811)
Henri I develops his vision of a prosperous and sovereign Haitian state, capable of demonstrating to the world the capacity of Black people to govern themselves and build a great civilization.
Alexandre Pétion — On Democracy and Land Reform (1809)
Pétion sets out the foundations of his progressive social policy, including the redistribution of land to former slaves and soldiers of the Revolution.
Jean Price-Mars — Thus Spoke the Uncle (1928)
Founding text of the Haitian Indigenist movement. Price-Mars calls on Haitian intellectuals to value African cultures and the Vodou tradition as the basis for an authentic national identity.
Jean-Bertrand Aristide — First Inaugural Address (1991)
Haiti’s first democratically elected president, Aristide expresses his vision of a just and equitable Haiti, founded on popular participation and the dignity of all citizens.

Resources

Consulting the Original Archives

To access original documents, researchers can consult the National Archives of Haiti in Port-au-Prince as well as the British National Archives in London, where a copy of the 1804 Act of Independence was found in 2010. Many American and European universities also hold collections of Haitian historical documents.

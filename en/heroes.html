Heroes of the Haitian Revolution

These historical figures dedicated their lives to the struggle for freedom and changed the course of world history. Their memory lives on in the heart of every Haitian.

Toussaint Louverture

1743 – 1803 – Father of the Revolution

A former enslaved man who became the greatest military and political leader of the Haitian Revolution, Toussaint Louverture was a brilliant strategist who successively defeated the Spanish, British and French armies. Captured through treachery by the French, he died in the Fort de Joux prison. His courage and vision paved the way for Haitian independence.

Jean-Jacques Dessalines

1758 – 1806 – Founding Father of the Nation

Born into slavery, he became the general who proclaimed Haiti's independence on January 1, 1804, in Gonaïves. Crowned Emperor Jacques I of Haiti, this unyielding military leader definitively defeated Napoleonic forces at Vertières. Assassinated in 1806, he remains the uncontested founding father of the Haitian nation.

Henri Christophe

1767 – 1820 – Builder King of the North

A general of the Revolution, Henri Christophe became King Henri I of northern Haiti. He is the builder of the majestic Citadelle Laferrière and the Sans-Souci Palace, both listed as UNESCO World Heritage Sites. A remarkable administrator, he sought to establish a prosperous and sovereign state capable of resisting any new invasion.

Alexandre Pétion

1770 – 1818 – President of the Republic of the South

President of the Republic of Haiti for the southern part of the country, Pétion was a statesman with deep democratic convictions. He implemented a generous land reform and supported Simón Bolívar by providing troops and weapons for the liberation of South America. He is remembered for his generosity and humanism.

Marie-Claire Heureuse Félicité

1758 – 1858 – Heroine and Symbol of Compassion

Wife of Jean-Jacques Dessalines, she was a heroine of the Revolution recognized for her courage, compassion and care for wounded soldiers. She is credited with saving many lives during the conflicts. A national symbol of strength and kindness, she is celebrated as one of the most important female figures in Haitian history.

Boisrond-Tonnerre

1776 – 1806 – Author of the Declaration of Independence

Secretary to Dessalines, Boisrond-Tonnerre was the author of the official text of the Declaration of Independence. His fiery pen and bold formulations make him a symbol of revolutionary defiance. He embodied the total and irreversible break with the French colonial order.

Sanité Belair

1781 – 1802 – First Female Officer of the Revolution

A revolutionary officer, Sanité Belair fought alongside her husband Charles Belair. Arrested by French forces, she was executed with admirable dignity, refusing to be blindfolded. She is considered one of the first female officers of any American revolution, and her courage remains a symbol of female bravery.

Capois la Mort

1766 – 1806 – The Hero of Vertières

A legendary general at the Battle of Vertières, Capois la Mort distinguished himself with extraordinary bravery. He charged enemy lines repeatedly despite his wounds, inspiring his troops with his absolute fearlessness. His nickname, meaning "Capois Death," speaks to his total disregard for danger — a quality that earned him admiration from fighters on both sides.

Northern Capital

Cap-Haïtien

Founded in 1670 as Cap-Français, former colonial capital of Saint-Domingue nicknamed "the Paris of the Antilles", Cap-Haïtien is the city where the Haitian Revolution began — and where independence was proclaimed.

Foundation & Colonial Grandeur

Cap-Haïtien, birthplace of the Revolution and independence

Cap-Français was founded in 1670 as a trading post on the northern coast of Saint-Domingue. By the end of the seventeenth century the city was growing at a pace that astonished contemporaries. By the mid-eighteenth century it was the most prosperous city in the Caribbean — perhaps in all colonial America. Travellers who disembarked on its grid-patterned streets discovered theatres, newspapers, Masonic lodges, lively cafés, private mansions adorned with ornate ironwork. French visitors nicknamed it "the Paris of the Antilles", not as a flattering metaphor but because its intellectual and social life genuinely rivalled that of a major French provincial city. Moreau de Saint-Méry, in his Description topographique de la partie française de Saint-Domingue (1797–1798), paints a portrait that oscillates between admiration and keen awareness that all of this rested on the forced labour of several hundred thousand enslaved people.

It was in the belt of plantations surrounding Le Cap that the Revolution took its first steps. On the night of 14–15 August 1791, at Bois Caïman — a place whose precise location historians still debate — a ceremony brought together hundreds of enslaved people under the leadership of Dutty Boukman and a Vodou priestess whose name varies across sources (Cécile Fatiman in several Haitian traditions). A few days later, on 22 August, the plantations of the North went up in flames. The smoke from burning sugar mills was visible from the Cap's wharves. The largest insurrection in the history of the Americas had begun.

In June 1793, the city was burned. Governor Galbaud, in conflict with civil commissioners Polverel and Sonthonax, released thousands of sailors who looted and burned much of the city. The commissioners, to counter this white mutiny, offered freedom to enslaved people in the surrounding area in exchange for their military support — a foreshadowing of the formal abolition that the Convention would proclaim in February 1794. The fire destroyed entire neighbourhoods, and the Cap-Français rebuilt afterwards was a partly new city, superimposed on the ruins of the colonial masterpiece.

The relationship between Bois Caïman and the Haitian Revolution has been the subject of numerous political, religious and popular instrumentalizations. Certain North American evangelical circles have presented this ceremony as a "pact with the devil" that would explain Haiti's misfortunes. Serious historians — C. L. R. James, David Geggus, Laurent Dubois — insist that Bois Caïman was above all an act of political and military mobilization, rooted in African religious traditions, and that the diabolizing caricature says infinitely more about the racism of its authors than about the reality of 1791.

1 January 1804: the proclamation of independence

It is at Gonaïves that Dessalines read the formal declaration of independence, but it is at Le Cap, renamed Cap-Haïtien, that the news resonated with the greatest symbolic force: this was the city that had seen the revolt born, that had burned, that had resisted, and that now welcomed the victorious general. On 1 January 1804, Haiti became the world's first Black republic and the second independent nation in the New World. The name "Haiti" is itself a restitution: it is the Taíno name of the island, Ayiti, "land of high mountains", chosen deliberately to break with any trace of the colonial name.

Henri Christophe and the Kingdom of the North

After Dessalines's death in 1806, Haiti divided. In the north, Henri Christophe governed — first as president, then as King Henri I from 1811. His reign used Cap-Haïtien as its administrative capital, but his monumental grandeur deployed itself at Milot, about twenty kilometres into the mountains. The Sans-Souci Palace, whose architectural sophistication astonished foreign visitors — ambassadors, journalists, merchants — was completed around 1813. The Citadelle Laferrière, a fortress perched at 900 metres altitude, is one of the most impressive military constructions of the pre-industrial era. These two monuments, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List in 1982, bear the collective name of the Historic National Park — Citadelle, Sans-Souci, Ramiers. Christophe shot himself with a golden bullet in 1820 during an uprising by his own troops. Reunification of the country under Boyer that same year.

Cap-Haïtien today

The nineteenth and twentieth centuries saw Le Cap gradually fade behind Port-au-Prince. The administrative, commercial and political centralization around the capital drained elites, capital and institutions southward. Yet Cap-Haïtien retains a strong identity, a marked regional pride, and a vibrant cultural tradition. Today, with around 300,000 inhabitants, it is the country's second city. Its network of colonial streets — largely intact, unlike Port-au-Prince devastated by earthquakes and fires — makes it an urban space of rare architectural coherence in Haiti. The city is the starting point for the Northern tourist circuit, which includes the Citadelle, the beaches of Cormier and the site of Labadee. Its cultural life — carnival, music, crafts — retains an intensity that the people of Le Cap assert with a certain pride, tinged with mild acidity towards the capital.

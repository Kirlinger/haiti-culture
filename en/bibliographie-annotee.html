Annotated Bibliography on Haiti

A reader's guide — not a simple list — for navigating the academic and literary literature on Haiti. Each entry explains what the work covers, its perspective, and who will find it most useful.

Home / Annotated Bibliography

Selection criteria. The works selected were chosen for their academic rigour, their centrality to Haitian studies, or their exceptional ability to make Haiti accessible to non-specialist readers. Priority is given to sources written by Haitian authors. The badges indicate the recommended reading level: Introductory for beginners and the general public, Intermediate for students and curious readers with some prior knowledge, Advanced for researchers and specialists. This guide is not exhaustive; it aims to orient, not to catalogue.

Category 1

Foundations of Haitian History

These works constitute the indispensable bedrock of Haitian historiography — foundational accounts written in the immediate aftermath of events through to modern syntheses that renew their reading. Any serious reader on the Haitian Revolution will eventually encounter each of these titles.

Histoire d'Haïti Advanced

Thomas Madiou · 8 volumes · Imprimerie de J. Courtois, Port-au-Prince, 1847–1848 (reprint: Henri Deschamps, 1987–1991)

The first major narrative history of Haiti written by a Haitian, Thomas Madiou's Histoire d'Haïti remains an indispensable monument for any researcher interested in the revolutionary period and the first decades of independence. Madiou writes within living memory of the events — he collects direct testimonies from participants in the Revolution — which gives the work the value of a primary source as much as an analysis. His account is inevitably partial: Madiou is a son of the mulatto elite and this colours his reading of Dessalines in particular. But the factual density, the breadth of chronological coverage (from 1492 to 1843) and the quality of the writing make it an irreplaceable reference. The 1980s reprint is more accessible than the nineteenth-century originals. To read Madiou is to hear a Haitian voice of the nineteenth century speaking to its contemporaries about what they had just lived through together.

Ideal for: researchers in Haitian history, comparative historians, specialists in Atlantic revolutions.

Études sur l'Histoire d'Haïti Advanced

Beaubrun Ardouin · 11 volumes · Dezobry et Magdeleine, Paris, 1853–1860

Ardouin, senator and diplomat, brings an institutional and political perspective that Madiou, more narrative in approach, does not develop with the same rigour. The two men cordially detested each other and disagreed on Dessalines: Madiou is more favourable to the nation's founder; Ardouin, representing the southern elite, tends to portray him more darkly. Reading both in parallel is a lesson in historical method — the same revolution seen by two Haitian contemporaries yields two profoundly different accounts. The Études cover the colonial period, the Revolution and the first post-independence decades with an attention to institutional detail that remains valuable for historians of law and institutions. All eleven volumes are dense and demand a patient reader. Ardouin is indispensable, not necessarily enjoyable.

Ideal for: researchers in Haitian political and institutional history, historians comparing foundational accounts.

The Haitian Revolution, 1789–1804 Introductory

Thomas Ott · University of Tennessee Press, 1973

A solid and reliable synthesis of the revolutionary period in English, useful for anglophone readers approaching the subject for the first time. Ott draws on the secondary sources available in his era and offers a clear chronological account of the main events from 1789 to 1804. The work lacks the analytical depth of James or the archival richness of Dubois, but has the advantage of clarity and concision. It remains an honest, well-constructed entry point for undergraduate history students. Since its publication, the historiography of the Haitian Revolution has been considerably enriched, and Ott should be supplemented rather than read in isolation.

Ideal for: anglophone undergraduates, secondary school teachers, journalists seeking rapid factual grounding.

The Black Jacobins: Toussaint L'Ouverture and the San Domingo Revolution Intermediate

C.L.R. James · Secker & Warburg, London, 1938 (revised edition: Vintage, New York, 1963)

It would be difficult to overstate the importance of this book. James, a Trinidadian Marxist intellectual, was the first to inscribe the Haitian Revolution in world history — not as a regional curiosity but as the most radical revolutionary event of its century, preceding and conditioning African decolonization. His analysis of Toussaint as a tragic figure — caught between revolutionary logic and his compromises with France — remains one of the most penetrating ever written. The 1963 appendix, composed at the hour of African independence, places the Haitian Revolution in direct dialogue with Nkrumah and national liberation struggles: it is a masterpiece in its own right. After 85 years, The Black Jacobins remains essential not only for understanding Haiti, but for understanding how the oppressed make history.

Ideal for: students of political science and history, readers interested in postcolonial and pan-African studies, any serious reader on the Revolution.

Avengers of the New World: The Story of the Haitian Revolution Introductory

Laurent Dubois · Belknap Press / Harvard University Press, Cambridge, 2004

The best modern synthesis of the Haitian Revolution in English, and probably the most accessible book for a broad educated audience. Dubois, an American historian specializing in the Atlantic world, works with French and Haitian archives to produce an account that is both rigorous and remarkably readable. He gives voice to actors often absent from traditional narratives — women, ordinary soldiers, free people of colour — and situates the revolution within the political and economic currents of the eighteenth-century Atlantic. The book avoids the two usual pitfalls: naive glorification and exoticizing condescension. It is an ideal starting point before approaching more analytically demanding texts like James.

Ideal for: general readers, undergraduates, teachers, journalists, anyone beginning to take an interest in Haiti.

Category 2

Post-Independence Haiti (19th–20th centuries)

The period 1804–1986 — from independence to the fall of Jean-Claude Duvalier — is covered by works of very uneven quality and accessibility. The works below constitute the most solid analytical references for understanding the country's long-term political and social trajectory.

From Dessalines to Duvalier: Race, Colour and National Independence in Haiti Intermediate

David Nicholls · Cambridge University Press, 1979 (revised edition: Macmillan, 1996)

This is the reference analytical work on Haitian politics from 1804 to the Duvalier era. Nicholls organizes his analysis around the question of colour — the divide between mulatto elites and the Black political class — and demonstrates how this tension structured ideology, alliances and political conflicts over a century and a half. No other single volume achieves this level of analytical rigour over such a long period. The 1996 revised edition incorporates historiographical debates of the 1980s–1990s. Nicholls is not Haitian, but his intellectual honesty and the subtlety of his analysis more than compensate for this distance. It is a book one rereads differently depending on when one opens it.

Ideal for: students in political science and modern history, researchers in Caribbean and African-American studies.

Éventail d'histoire vivante d'Haïti Advanced

Leslie Manigat · 5 volumes · CHUDAC, Port-au-Prince, 2001–2007

A monument of political erudition by a man who was both a serious historian and an actor in Haitian history — Leslie Manigat was president of Haiti for a few months in 1988, then found himself in exile. This dual position — the scholar and the witness — gives his work a depth and sometimes a singular tension: one senses that the historian cannot always entirely detach himself from the politician. The five volumes cover political history, institutions, social classes and parties with impressive encyclopaedic density. Access to these volumes remains difficult outside Haiti and certain specialist university libraries. For the reader who finds them, it is a mine.

Ideal for: specialist historians, researchers in Haitian political science, readers with an already solid knowledge of the subject.

Note on the political context of sources. Several reference works on Haitian history were written in exile: Manigat wrote from Caracas and Trinidad, Suzy Castor from Mexico. This context inevitably shapes their relationship to available sources and their positioning in Haitian internal debates. This is not a reason to disqualify them — it is a reason to read them with awareness.

Histoire du peuple haïtien (1492–1952) Intermediate

Dantès Bellegarde · Lausanne, 1953

Dantès Bellegarde is the great diplomat-historian of twentieth-century Haiti — Haiti's representative to the League of Nations, a respected figure on the international stage. His Histoire du peuple haïtien is a synthesis written for a cultivated international audience, with the aim of presenting Haiti with dignity and rigour in a context where external representations were often condescending or caricatural. The tone is sometimes slightly apologetic — Bellegarde seeks to persuade a Western reader — but the analytical quality remains high. It is a precious window onto what the Haitian elite thought of itself and its history in the mid-twentieth century. Read alongside Trouillot to measure its blind spots.

Ideal for: students of diplomatic history, readers interested in Haiti's representation in the context of decolonization.

Haiti in the World Economy: Class, Race and Underdevelopment since 1700 Intermediate

Alex Dupuy · Westview Press, Boulder, 1989

Dupuy, a Haitian-American sociologist, applies dependency theory to Haitian political and economic history from the colonial period. His central argument is that Haitian poverty is not the product of internal cultural factors but of its structural insertion into the world economy — the colonial debt, American occupation, extractive investments. This is the best available analysis of the mechanisms by which external pressures have shaped internal politics. The theoretical framework is clearly marked, but the empirical argumentation is solid. A necessary counterweight to culturalist readings of the Haitian situation.

Ideal for: students of political economy, development sociology, Caribbean studies.

Category 3

Society, Culture and Anthropology

Haitian anthropology produces some of the most innovative work in Caribbean studies. This category brings together works on popular culture, Vodou, the peasantry and social structures — a domain where both Haitians and foreigners have produced foundational work.

Ainsi parla l'oncle : Essais d'ethnographie Intermediate

Jean Price-Mars · Imprimerie de Compiègne, 1928 (reprint: Leméac, Montréal, 1973)

This book changed the way Haitian intellectuals thought about their own culture. Price-Mars, a physician and diplomat trained in Paris, published in 1928 an ethnographic manifesto that rehabilitates Vodou, peasant practices and the African heritage against the contempt of the Francophone Haitian elites who defined themselves essentially in relation to France. The central concept — the collective bovarism of an elite that mistakes itself for what it is not — remains one of the most accurate formulations of Haitian identity schizophrenia. The Oncle directly inspired the Négritude of Césaire and Senghor. It is a founding text not only for Haiti but for twentieth-century Afro-diasporic thought.

Ideal for: students in cultural anthropology, postcolonial studies, Francophone literature, history of ideas.

Le Vaudou haïtien Intermediate

Alfred Métraux · Gallimard, Paris, 1958 (Bibliothèque des sciences humaines)

The most rigorous ethnographic study ever produced on Haitian Vodou remains this book by a Swiss anthropologist who spent several years in Haiti, notably in the Marbial valley, in collaboration with the Haitian ethnologist Lorimer Denis. Métraux describes with precision the rituals, the lwa, the ceremonies, the cult objects and the social structures of Vodou without the exoticizing condescension or romantic fascination that characterize many contemporary studies. The book shows Vodou as a coherent religious system, rooted in history and in precise social conditions. Despite its age, it remains the standard reference — any researcher on Vodou must start here.

Ideal for: students in religious anthropology, researchers on African-American religions, anyone who wants to understand Vodou seriously.

Mama Lola: A Vodou Priestess in Brooklyn Introductory

Karen McCarthy Brown · University of California Press, Berkeley, 1991

Brown, an American anthropologist, follows over two decades a Haitian mambo — Mama Lola, born Alourdes Margaux — in her temple in the Flatbush neighbourhood of Brooklyn. The method is deliberately immersive: Brown ends up being initiated and becoming a practitioner herself. This methodological choice, which sparked debate in the discipline, produces an extraordinary book on what Vodou concretely means in people's lives — how it manages anxieties, bonds families, gives meaning to hardship. It is the best introduction to the lived dimension of Vodou, particularly in the context of the Haitian American diaspora. More accessible than Métraux, more grounded in practice than most theoretical studies.

Ideal for: general readers, anthropology students, teachers seeking to approach Vodou in a non-caricatural way.

Caribbean Transformations Advanced

Sidney Mintz · Aldine, Chicago, 1974

Mintz, an American anthropologist specializing in the Caribbean and co-author with Eric Wolf of foundational work on plantations, brings together in this collection analyses that shed indirect but essential light on the Haitian peasant economy. The chapter on rural Caribbean markets and the role of women in trade remains one of the best structural analyses of the informal economy in the Caribbean. To understand the Haitian peasant world — the madan sara, the markets, polyculture — Mintz offers conceptual tools that few others have equalled. This is not a book about Haiti alone, but it is more useful for understanding Haiti than many Haiti-specific books.

Ideal for: anthropologists, economists interested in informal markets, researchers in comparative Caribbean studies.

Category 4

The Question of Race and Identity

Haiti is at the heart of some of the most important reflections on race, identity and history in Atlantic thought. These works address directly the way race has been constructed, instrumentalized and contested — and how Haiti is systematically misrepresented in Western historiography.

De l'égalité des races humaines : Anthropologie positive Intermediate

Anténor Firmin · F. Pichon, Paris, 1885

Published in Paris in 1885, this book is a rigorous and courageous scientific refutation of Gobineau's theses on the hierarchy of races, written by a 35-year-old Haitian diplomat living in the capital of colonial France. Firmin mobilizes data from physical anthropology, linguistics, history and archaeology to demonstrate the absence of any scientific basis for the racist theories then dominant in European academic circles. He precedes Franz Boas's work by several years. The work was largely ignored by the Western academy — proof by example of his own thesis — and was only rediscovered at the end of the twentieth century thanks in particular to the work of anthropologist Carolyn Fluehr-Lobban. Reading it in 2025 remains a lesson in epistemological humility.

Ideal for: philosophers, anthropologists, historians of ideas, students in racial and postcolonial studies.

Silencing the Past: Power and the Production of History Intermediate

Michel-Rolph Trouillot · Beacon Press, Boston, 1995

This book redefined the way historians think about historical production in the anglophone social sciences. Trouillot, a Haitian anthropologist teaching in Chicago then at Johns Hopkins, starts from a simple question: why is the Haitian Revolution systematically under-represented, misunderstood or simply absent from major Western historical narratives, even though it is objectively one of the most important revolutionary events of the modern era? His answer — that history is produced at moments of silence, in archives, narratives and commemorations controlled by those who hold power — has become a reference theoretical framework throughout critical historiography. The chapter comparing the Alamo and the Haitian Revolution in American memory is of striking acuity. Trouillot is the most-cited Haitian thinker in anglophone humanities.

Ideal for: any engaged reader — historians, anthropologists, sociologists, professors — who wants to understand why Haiti is poorly known and why that is not an accident.

L'occupation américaine d'Haïti Intermediate

Suzy Castor · Henri Deschamps, Port-au-Prince, 1988

Suzy Castor, Haitian historian and founder of CRESFED (Centre de Recherche et de Formation Économique et Sociale pour le Développement), offers in this work the Haitian reference analysis of the American occupation of 1915–1934. She works systematically with Haitian archives rarely exploited in anglophone studies of the same period. Her analytical framework is clearly anchored in an anti-imperialist perspective, but the documentation is solid and the conclusions withstand critical scrutiny. The American occupation is often studied from an American point of view or in a purely military manner; Castor shows its effects on Haitian society, the peasantry and internal political structures. To be read as a counterpoint to American studies of the period.

Ideal for: historians of international relations, specialists in American history in the Caribbean, researchers in postcolonial studies.

La culture opprimée Advanced

Jean Casimir · Lakansièl, Port-au-Prince, 1981

Jean Casimir, Haitian sociologist and diplomat, develops in this complex and demanding work an original thesis: Haitian peasant culture should not be read as the residue of an incapacity for development, but as a structured form of resistance to the domination of the state and the elites. Haitian peasants have developed over the centuries strategies of survival and autonomy — economic, spiritual, linguistic — that constitute a counter-culture in the full sense of the term. This framework, close to James Scott's concept of the weapons of the weak, offers a radically different reading from that of development economists. The book is demanding and sometimes arduous, but rewards the patient reader with a vision that profoundly renews understanding of Haitian society.

Ideal for: sociologists, anthropologists, researchers in resistance studies and alternative development.

Category 5

Economy and Development

The literature on the Haitian economy is dominated by foreign authors — economists from multilateral institutions, American researchers, physician-anthropologists. This is in itself revealing and should be read as a symptom as much as a source of information.

The scarcity of Haitian voices in economics and medicine. It is striking to note that the two fields where Haitian academic production is thinnest are precisely those that most directly determine the living conditions of the majority: development economics and public health. This gap is not cultural — it is the result of decades of brain drain, underfunding of Haitian universities and an international aid system that often prefers to send its own experts rather than fund the training of local researchers. The works below, almost all written from the outside, should be read with this awareness.

The Uses of Haiti Introductory

Paul Farmer · Common Courage Press, Monroe (ME), 1994 (3rd revised ed., 2006)

Paul Farmer, physician and medical anthropologist co-founder of Partners in Health, has spent more time in Haiti than most of the "experts" who write about it. The Uses of Haiti is a documented and polemical critique of the way American policy — from the 1915 occupation to the interventions of the 1990s — has systematically shaped Haitian poverty to its own advantage. The book is openly partisan, and Farmer does not conceal this. But his argumentation is empirically grounded and his sources are impeccable. It is the most effective book for understanding the link between American foreign policy and Haitian underdevelopment, and for deconstructing the narrative that Haitian poverty is endogenous. Essential reading before any report on international aid in Haiti.

Ideal for: general readers, journalists, development practitioners, students in international health and American studies.

The Prophet and Power: Jean-Bertrand Aristide, the International Community, and Haiti Intermediate

Alex Dupuy · Rowman & Littlefield, Lanham, 2007

The most rigorous analysis of the Aristide years and international intervention in Haiti in the 1990s–2000s. Dupuy, at once an initial sympathizer of Aristide and a critic of his later compromises, produces a work that avoids both usual pitfalls: neither the hagiography of a left that saw Aristide as a messiah, nor the demonization of an American right that treated him as a threat. He analyzes how international conditionality constrained the available political options and how Aristide navigated — and sometimes capitulated — in this constrained space. The conclusion on the structural limits of any progressive politics in a country under strong external dependency is of a lucidity that extends far beyond the Haitian case.

Ideal for: political scientists, researchers in Caribbean-US international relations, students in political development.

Haiti: Toward a New Narrative Introductory

World Bank · Washington D.C., 2015 (Systematic Country Diagnostic)

An institutional diagnostic document produced by the World Bank, this work is valuable primarily for its updated statistical data on poverty, inequalities, access to services, governance and remittance flows. One should not expect a critical analysis of international aid structures — the World Bank does not self-critique in its official diagnostics — but the compiled figures are useful as a factual starting point for any researcher or journalist. To be read with a critical eye towards the narrative framework surrounding it: the rhetoric of a "new narrative" often masks a re-enactment of the same prescriptions. Farmer or Dupuy will provide the necessary distance.

Ideal for: journalists seeking data, development practitioners, students looking for a statistical overview of the Haitian situation.

Category 6

Haitian Literature

Haitian literature is one of the richest in the Francophone world and yet one of the least read outside Haiti and the diaspora. It documents, as no academic discipline could, the intimate experience of violence, exile, identity and resistance.

The translation chasm. The majority of Haitian literature in French — and almost all literature in Haitian Creole — has not been translated into English. This means that the anglophone reader has access to only a fraction of Haitian literary production, essentially filtered by the choices of American publishers and diaspora authors already integrated into Anglo-Saxon circuits. Jacques Stephen Alexis, René Depestre, Jean Métellus and many others are virtually unknown to the anglophone public. This translation gap is a political act as much as an editorial accident.

Gouverneurs de la Rosée Introductory

Jacques Roumain · Les Éditeurs Français Réunis, Paris, 1944

The most celebrated Haitian novel, translated into at least twenty languages — including an English version by Langston Hughes himself — and written in three weeks by an author who would die at 37. Manuel, a migrant peasant returned from a long exile in Cuba, seeks to reconcile two families torn apart by an old quarrel in a thirsty village on the Cul-de-Sac plain. Roumain weaves the lyricism of a stylized Haitian Creole, the symbolic density of earth and water, and a clearly Marxist political consciousness without the novel ever becoming a tract. It is a masterpiece in the proper sense: a book that says something true about the human condition while remaining profoundly Haitian. An ideal starting point for anyone wishing to discover Haitian literature.

Ideal for: all readers; Francophone literature courses, secondary schools, universities, book clubs.

Compère Général Soleil Intermediate

Jacques Stephen Alexis · Gallimard, Paris, 1955 (coll. "La Croix du Sud")

Alexis's epic novel — physician, communist and future martyr of the Duvalierist dictatorship — follows the journey of Hilarion Hilarius, a young Haitian from the slums of Port-au-Prince, through the Great Depression, Haitian politics of the 1930s and the Dominican army's massacre of Haitians in 1937, which killed between 15,000 and 20,000 people in a matter of days. Alexis invents a realism he himself calls "marvellous realism" — a way of weaving everyday Haitian life, Vodou and class struggle into a total narrative. He disappeared in 1961, captured by Duvalier's Tonton Macoutes while attempting to re-enter Haiti clandestinely. This novel is a testimony as much as a fiction.

Ideal for: readers with some grounding in Haitian history; courses on Caribbean literature, the 1937 massacres, Haitian-Dominican relations.

Breath, Eyes, Memory & The Farming of Bones Introductory

Edwidge Danticat · Soho Press, New York, 1994 & 1998

Danticat is the most widely known voice of the anglophone Haitian diaspora. Born in Port-au-Prince and partly raised in Brooklyn, she writes in English for an American audience while remaining deeply rooted in Haitian experience. Breath, Eyes, Memory, her first novel, explores the intergenerational transmission of trauma through a Haitian family between Haiti and New York. The Farming of Bones is her most historically ambitious work: a reconstruction of the Dominican massacre of 1937 as seen by a Haitian domestic worker in the Dominican Republic. Emotionally devastating, accessible, internationally acclaimed. An ideal gateway for the anglophone reader into the contemporary Haitian experience.

Ideal for: general anglophone readers, American high schools and universities, book clubs, anyone wishing to understand the Haitian diaspora through fiction.

Comment faire l'amour avec un Nègre sans se fatiguer & L'énigme du retour Introductory

Dany Laferrière · VLB éditeur, Montréal, 1985 & Grasset, Paris, 2009 (Prix Médicis)

Laferrière is the most celebrated living Haitian writer on the international Francophone scene — a member of the Académie française since 2013, the first Canadian to sit there. His first novel, provocative and funny, caused a scandal in Montréal in 1985. But it is L'énigme du retour, published twenty-four years later, that constitutes his most mature work: a long prose poem on the return to Haiti following his father's death, on aging, exile and what remains of an identity divided among several countries and several languages. The title plays on the title of his uncle Davertige's poem and on the return of the Prodigal Son. It is a meditation on belonging that speaks to every exile, Haitian or not.

Ideal for: general Francophone readers, prose poetry readers, anyone interested in diasporic identity and exile.

Hadriana dans tous mes rêves Introductory

René Depestre · Gallimard, Paris, 1988 (Prix Renaudot)

Depestre, Haitian poet and novelist who lived in Castro's Cuba and in the French Lot, offers with this novel a love letter to Jacmel, the Haitian city of carnival and festivals. The novel follows Hadriana, a young French woman transformed into a zombie on her wedding day, through a narrative of magical realism that draws as much from the Vodou tradition as from surrealism. Depestre makes it a sensual and melancholy celebration of Haitian culture at its most vital — festival, desire, death made familiar. It is a novel of pleasure as much as a cultural document: a rare combination in literature about Haiti.

Ideal for: fans of magical realism, readers interested in Jacmel's culture, general Francophone readers.

Category 7

The Haitian Diaspora

The Haitian diaspora — estimated at between 1.5 and 2 million people in the United States alone — sends more money to Haiti each year than total international aid. Diaspora studies constitute a rapidly growing field, at the intersection of sociology, anthropology and migration studies.

Georges Woke Up Laughing: Long-Distance Nationalism and the Search for Home Intermediate

Nina Glick Schiller & Georges Eugène Fouron · Duke University Press, Durham, 2001

This is the best ethnographic account of Haitian transnationalism. Glick Schiller, an American anthropologist, co-writes the book with her principal informant turned co-author — Georges Fouron, a Haitian settled in New York — a methodological choice that brings the book closer to the co-production that Haitian researchers might have produced alone. The book follows the Fouron family between New York and Haiti over several years and theorizes the concept of "long-distance nationalism": the way migrants maintain a political and affective belonging to their country of origin while building their lives elsewhere. It is an analysis of dual loyalty, fractured belonging and citizenship as a transnational practice. A classic of migration studies.

Ideal for: sociologists, anthropologists, migration specialists, students in Caribbean-American studies.

Haitian Immigrants in Black America: A Sociological and Sociolinguistic Portrait Intermediate

Flore Zéphir · Bergin & Garvey, Westport, 1996

Zéphir, a Haitian-American sociolinguist, examines how Haitian immigrants negotiate their identity within the African American community — a low-visibility but real terrain of tension, where differences in language, religion, relationships to "Blackness" and history create complex frictions and solidarities. The study, based on fieldwork in several Haitian American communities, documents the strategies of integration, distinction and invisibilization adopted by migrants. It is one of the rare works to address the Haitian diaspora in its internal relationships to the African American community rather than solely in its relationship to dominant white society.

Ideal for: sociologists, specialists in African American studies, researchers on Caribbean migration in the United States.

Category 8

Online Resources and Archives

Digital resources have radically transformed access to primary sources on Haiti. Several major collections are now available online, even if gaps remain significant — particularly for Haiti's internal archives, many of which were damaged or lost in successive disasters.

Bibliothèque Nationale de la République d'Haïti (BNRH)

Port-au-Prince · bnrhaiti.org

The BNRH is Haiti's national legal deposit and holds a unique collection of Haitian publications since the nineteenth century. Its digitized collection, still partial, is progressively coming online. The holdings include old Haitian newspapers, political pamphlets, music scores and official documents unavailable elsewhere. Physical access to the library remains the primary method for researchers working on non-digitized Haitian sources. The collection suffered damage in the 2010 earthquake; reconstruction and digitization efforts continue.

Ideal for: researchers working on Haitian primary sources from the 19th–20th centuries.

Digital Library of the Caribbean (dLOC)

International consortium · dloc.com

dLOC is the largest digital collection of historical Caribbean sources, including a substantial portion of Haitian holdings: nineteenth-century newspapers, maps, photographs, official documents, audio recordings. The collection is the result of a partnership between the University of Florida, the State University of Haiti and dozens of Caribbean partner institutions. Any researcher working on Haiti before physically visiting the archives should explore dLOC — it contains sources that would once have required a trip to Port-au-Prince, Kingston or Paris. The search interface is effective and access is free.

Ideal for: any researcher or student seeking digitized Haitian primary sources.

Bibliothèque Haïtienne des Pères du Saint-Esprit (BHPSE)

Port-au-Prince, Haiti

An exceptional archival collection held by the Spiritan congregation in Port-au-Prince, the BHPSE holds a collection of colonial-era manuscripts, ecclesiastical documents and sources on the social and religious history of Haiti since the eighteenth century. Access is by appointment and reserved for accredited researchers. The collection is not fully catalogued and part of it remains little explored in academic literature. Researchers working on Haitian Catholicism, missions and rural social history will find irreplaceable sources here.

Ideal for: historians of religion, researchers on colonial history and nineteenth-century Haiti.

Haitian Collections at Yale University

Yale University Library, New Haven · library.yale.edu

Yale holds one of the most important collections of nineteenth-century Haitian sources accessible in the United States, particularly for diplomatic correspondences, Haitian official publications and the holdings of travellers and foreign observers of the era. The Beinecke Rare Book & Manuscript Library holds valuable documents on the revolutionary and post-independence period. The collections are partially digitized and accessible online via Yale's catalogue.

Ideal for: North American researchers working on Haitian diplomatic and political history of the nineteenth century.

Specialized Academic Journals

JSTOR · Project MUSE · Access varies by institution

Two journals are particularly important for research on Haiti. The Journal of Haitian Studies (founded 1995, University of California at Santa Barbara) is the anglophone reference academic journal for Haitian studies, covering history, literature, anthropology and political science. Caribbean Quarterly (University of the West Indies) offers a broader Caribbean perspective but regularly publishes quality work on Haiti. For Francophone sources, Conjonction — a Franco-Haitian journal published in Port-au-Prince — is a valuable tool for literature and culture, with a long continuity since 1946.

Ideal for: researchers and students seeking recent academic articles on Haiti.

Reference Guide

Welcome to the Haiti Encyclopedia

This encyclopedia offers an accessible reference guide on the essential topics of the history, geography, culture and politics of the Republic of Haiti. Arranged in alphabetical order, these articles serve as a first introduction to Haitian realities for any reader wishing to learn more about this fascinating country.

From A to Z

Encyclopedia Articles

A

Artibonite

Haiti's largest department and the country's agricultural breadbasket. The Artibonite River, Haiti's longest, supports large-scale rice cultivation. Saint-Marc and Gonaïves are the main cities of this department, vital to national food security.

B

Bois Caïman

Site of the ceremony on August 14, 1791 that ignited the Haitian Revolution. Led by Dutty Boukman and a mambo (Vodou priestess), this ritual ceremony united hundreds of enslaved people in an oath of resistance. Bois Caïman is a sacred site in Haitian collective memory.

C

Carnival

Annual popular festival that precedes Catholic Lent. Every city in Haiti organizes its own festivities with music, costumes, floats and dances. The Jacmel Carnival is famous for its handcrafted papier-mâché masks, of remarkable creativity and beauty.

D

Dessalines, Jean-Jacques

Founding father and first ruler of independent Haiti. He proclaimed independence on January 1, 1804 at Gonaïves and became Emperor Jacques I of Haiti. Born into slavery, he definitively defeated Napoleon's forces. Assassinated in 1806, he remains Haiti's most venerated national figure.

E

2010 Earthquake

On January 12, 2010, a magnitude 7.0 earthquake struck the Port-au-Prince region. More than 200,000 people lost their lives and 1.5 million were left homeless. One of the deadliest natural disasters in modern history, it durably weakened Haiti's social and economic fabric.

F

Fort-Liberté

Historic city in north-eastern Haiti, founded by the French as Fort-Dauphin. Site of important events during the colonial period and the Revolution. Today the capital of the Nord-Est department.

G

Gonaïves

The City of Independence, where Dessalines proclaimed Haiti's independence on January 1, 1804. A flagship city of the Artibonite, Gonaïves is a symbol of national freedom and the pilgrimage site for all January 1st commemorations.

H

Hispaniola

Island of the Greater Antilles shared between Haiti to the west and the Dominican Republic to the east. The second largest island in the Caribbean with an area of 76,480 km². Christopher Columbus landed here on December 5, 1492 on his first voyage.

I

Independence

Haiti declared its independence from France on January 1, 1804, becoming the first Black Republic in the world and the second independent country in the Americas, after the United States. This unique event in human history inspired emancipation movements around the world.

J

Jacmel

Beautiful coastal city in south-eastern Haiti, renowned for its preserved colonial architecture, dynamic art scene and unique papier-mâché Carnival. Designated a UNESCO Creative City, Jacmel is one of the country's most captivating destinations.

K

Kreyòl Ayisyen

The Haitian Creole language is spoken by virtually all Haitians. Born during the colonial period from the fusion of French with West African languages, it was recognized as an official language of Haiti in 1987. Literature and music in Creole have flourished remarkably since then.

L

Laferrière, Citadelle

Monumental fortress listed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, built by Henri Christophe between 1805 and 1820 in northern Haiti. The largest fortress in the Americas, perched at 900 metres altitude. A symbol of Haitian sovereignty and determination never to be reconquered.

M

Milot

City in northern Haiti, close to the Citadelle Laferrière and the Sans-Souci Palace. These two monuments form the National Historical Park, inscribed on the UNESCO World Heritage List since 1982. An essential site for understanding the history and greatness of the Haitian nation.

N

Négritude

A 20th-century cultural and intellectual movement celebrating African identity, history and culture. Haitian intellectuals such as Jean Price-Mars and Caribbean thinkers like Aimé Césaire were pioneers of this movement, which profoundly influenced African and Caribbean literary and political thought.

O

Ouanaminthe

Border city in north-eastern Haiti, facing Dajabón in the Dominican Republic. An important bilateral commercial hub with a very active cross-border market. The city is also known for its role during the Haitian Revolution.

P

Port-au-Prince

Capital and largest city of Haiti, founded in 1749. The political, economic and cultural centre of the country, seat of the government and the main national institutions. Severely damaged by the devastating earthquake of January 12, 2010, the city has been undergoing reconstruction since then.

R

Haitian Revolution

The only successful slave revolution in history, waged from 1791 to 1804. This extraordinary struggle resulted in Haiti's independence and inspired liberation movements around the world. It remains one of the most significant events in the universal history of human rights.

S

Sans-Souci Palace

Royal palace built by Henri Christophe in the early decades of the 19th century. Today a magnificent romantic ruin, it is included in the UNESCO World Heritage Site near Milot. A testament to Henri Christophe's ambition and vision for nation-building.

T

Toussaint Louverture

A former enslaved man turned military and political genius, he led the early phases of the Haitian Revolution. Governor of Saint-Domingue under the Constitution of 1801, he was treacherously captured by the French and died in prison at Fort de Joux in 1803. His vision and sacrifice made Haiti's independence possible.

U

State University of Haiti

Founded in 1944, the State University of Haiti is the country's main public university. Located in Port-au-Prince, it includes faculties of medicine, law, science and arts. Despite limited resources, it trains the country's intellectual and professional elites.

V

Vodou

Haitian religion that fuses the spiritual traditions of West Africa with Catholicism and indigenous beliefs. Officially recognized by the Haitian government as a religion in 2003, Vodou plays a central role in Haitian culture, art, music and social life.

W

Wyclef Jean

World-renowned Haitian-American musician, who rose to fame with the Fugees in the 1990s. Born in Croix-des-Bouquets, Haiti, he is one of the country's most celebrated cultural ambassadors and an active advocate for Haiti's development.
